== The one-line pitch

A practitioner's field guide to performance testing: how to model a workload,
run a load test that tells the truth, read the results without fooling yourself,
and wire the whole thing into CI/CD so performance stops being a fire drill and
becomes a property you can measure on every commit.

== Why this book, why now

Two things have changed in the last five years, and no current book sits at
their intersection.

First, *the tooling moved.* The center of gravity shifted from GUI-driven,
JVM-heavy tools (LoadRunner, classic JMeter) to lightweight, scriptable,
version-controlled load generators — k6, Gatling, Locust, Vegeta, wrk, and a new
generation of single-binary tools like loadr. Tests are code now. They live next
to the application, run in a pipeline, and gate a merge. Most books on the shelf
predate that shift and still teach the old workflow.

Second, *the culture moved.* SRE went mainstream. Teams now speak in SLOs and
error budgets. "Is it fast enough?" became a quantitative question with a
contractual answer. Yet the average engineer still runs a load test, sees a
number, and has no idea whether that number is honest — coordinated omission,
open-vs-closed workload models, and the tyranny of the average are still
routinely getting people fired... metaphorically, and occasionally not.

This book teaches performance testing the way it's actually practiced in 2026:
tool-agnostic on principles, opinionated on method, and honest about the
statistics. It uses concrete, runnable examples throughout — primarily with
*loadr* (a modern single-binary load tester) because its scenario model and
built-in analysis make the concepts legible, with side-by-side snippets in *k6*,
*JMeter*, *Gatling*, and *Locust* so the reader can carry the ideas to whatever
tool their shop already runs.

== Who it's for

The primary reader is a *software engineer, SRE, or QA/performance engineer* who
can write code and read a metrics dashboard but has never been taught
performance testing as a discipline. They can copy a k6 script off a blog; they
cannot yet tell you why their p99 is a lie, why their load generator is the
actual bottleneck, or how to size capacity from a soak test.

Secondary readers: *engineering managers and tech leads* who need to set SLOs
and defend capacity decisions to a budget owner; *DevOps/platform engineers*
building performance gates into pipelines.

Assumed background: comfortable on the command line, can read YAML/JSON and a
little JavaScript, understands HTTP. No prior performance-testing knowledge
assumed. No statistics background assumed — the book teaches the ~5 statistical
ideas that matter and refuses to teach the rest.

== What's already out there (and the gap)

[cols="2,3,2",options="header"]
|===
| Book | Strength | What it misses
| _The Art of Application Performance Testing_ (Molyneaux) | Solid on process and planning | Predates code-as-tests, CI gating, cloud-scale generation, OTel
| _Systems Performance_ (Gregg) | The definitive bottleneck-analysis reference | It's an observability/OS book, not a *testing* book — assumes you already have load
| _Site Reliability Engineering_ (Google) | Defined SLOs/error budgets for a generation | Not about testing at all; leaves "how do I generate and measure the load" as an exercise
| Tool docs (k6, Gatling, JMeter) | Accurate, current | Teach the tool, not the discipline; no cross-tool judgment, thin on statistics and workload modeling
|===

*The gap:* there is no current, tool-agnostic, engineer-facing book that takes
you from "why does performance matter" through "here is a correct, automated,
production-representative test whose results you can trust and defend." This book
fills exactly that gap.

== Specifications

* *Estimated length:* ~350 print pages / ~110,000 words.
* *Chapters:* 17 across five parts, plus three appendices.
* *Format:* AsciiDoc (O'Reilly Atlas).
* *Code:* every runnable example in an accompanying Git repo, CI-tested so the
  listings can't rot. Primary examples in loadr; comparison snippets in
  k6/JMeter/Gatling/Locust.
* *Estimated timeline:* full manuscript in ~7 months from contract at one
  chapter every ~10 days plus tech-review passes.

== Comprehensive outline (the topic list)

Each chapter below lists its abstract and the concrete topics it covers. This is
the "cover all the angles" spine: foundations, design, execution, analysis, and
operationalization.

=== Part I — Foundations

==== Chapter 1. Why Performance Is a Feature
*Abstract:* Performance framed as a product and business property, not a
late-stage QA gate. The economic case, the human-perception case, and why the
tail — not the average — is what users actually feel.
Topics: latency and revenue (the classic Amazon/Google/Akamai numbers and how to
reason about your own); tail latency and why one slow request in a fan-out poisons
a whole page; the psychology of waiting (100ms / 1s / 10s thresholds); performance
as reliability (timeouts, retries, and how slow becomes down); where performance
testing sits relative to functional testing, profiling, and observability; a taxonomy
of test types (load, stress, spike, soak/endurance, capacity, breakpoint,
scalability) and when each answers a real question.

==== Chapter 2. The Language of Performance
*Abstract:* The vocabulary and the five statistical ideas that everything else
depends on. Get these wrong and every later chapter lies to you.
Topics: latency vs response time vs service time; throughput and requests-per-second;
concurrency, and the difference between *virtual users* and *arrival rate*; Little's
Law and using it as a sanity check; percentiles done properly (why the mean and even
the median hide the pain; why you can't average percentiles); histograms and HDR
histograms; *coordinated omission* — the single most common way load tests lie, with
a worked example; saturation, utilization, and the knee of the curve; error rate and
what counts as an error; the USE and RED methods as mental models; SLIs, SLOs, SLAs,
Apdex, and error budgets.

=== Part II — Designing the Test

==== Chapter 3. Modeling the Workload
*Abstract:* A load test is a hypothesis about how the system will be used. This
chapter is about making that hypothesis realistic.
Topics: open vs closed workload models and why choosing wrong invalidates the run;
arrival-rate (throughput-driven) vs virtual-user (concurrency-driven) load; think
time and pacing; Poisson arrivals and burstiness; deriving a workload from production
telemetry (access logs, APM, request mix); the "busy hour" and seasonal peaks;
Markov-style user journeys and scenario weighting; steady-state vs ramp vs spike
profiles.

==== Chapter 4. Objectives, Baselines, and Success Criteria
*Abstract:* A test with no pass/fail criterion is a science-fair project. How to
turn "fast enough" into numbers a machine can check.
Topics: turning product/UX requirements into SLOs; choosing the right percentile and
window; setting a defensible baseline and detecting regression against it; error
budgets as a testing budget; latency *and* throughput *and* error-rate gates together;
budgets for cost and resource, not just time; writing criteria that fail loudly in CI.

==== Chapter 5. Test Data and the Environment
*Abstract:* The two things that quietly wreck most load tests: unrealistic data
and an unrepresentative environment.
Topics: parameterization and data feeders (CSV, JSON, generated, database-backed);
correlation and dynamic values (tokens, CSRF, session IDs, IDs minted mid-flow);
data volume and cardinality effects (a test DB with 100 rows lies); test-environment
fidelity and prod-like sizing; the "one-tenth environment" trap and how to scale
findings; seeding and teardown; data privacy, synthetic data, and PII; adversarial
and edge-case inputs (leading into complexity/DoS testing later).

==== Chapter 6. Designing Realistic Scenarios
*Abstract:* From a single request to a believable population of users.
Topics: modeling user journeys as scenarios; groups/transactions and why you name
them; weighting scenarios to match real traffic mix; ramps, stages, and arrival-rate
schedules; checks and assertions inside a flow; parmeterizing per-VU state; keeping
scenarios readable and reviewable as code.

=== Part III — Executing Tests

==== Chapter 7. The Tooling Landscape
*Abstract:* An honest, opinionated survey. What each major tool is good at, what
it costs you, and how to choose.
Topics: a decision framework (protocol coverage, scripting model, scale model,
reporting, CI-friendliness, cost, team skills); deep-enough looks at JMeter, Gatling,
k6, Locust, Vegeta/wrk, Artillery, Tsung, and loadr; GUI vs code vs hybrid; the JVM
tax; single-binary and containerized tools; when a "worse" tool is the right call
because your team already knows it.

==== Chapter 8. Your First Real Load Test
*Abstract:* Hands-on. Build a correct test from zero against a sample service,
then read what it tells you.
Topics: installing and running loadr; anatomy of a scenario file; a first HTTP test;
adding think time, ramps, and checks; the same test expressed in k6 and JMeter for
portability; reading the summary output honestly; common first-timer mistakes (testing
localhost, no warm-up, closed-model-by-accident) and how to catch them.

==== Chapter 9. Beyond HTTP: Advanced Protocols and Patterns
*Abstract:* Real systems aren't just REST. Testing everything else.
Topics: HTTP/2 and HTTP/3 nuances; gRPC and streaming; WebSocket and SSE; GraphQL
(and why query shape matters); message queues and event-driven systems (Kafka, AMQP);
database and cache load (SQL, Redis, Mongo); browser-level vs protocol-level testing
and when the difference matters; authentication at scale (OAuth token minting, JWTs,
refresh storms).

==== Chapter 10. Generating Load at Scale
*Abstract:* When one machine isn't enough — and how to make sure the machine
isn't the thing you're actually measuring.
Topics: recognizing when the *load generator* is the bottleneck (CPU, sockets, GC,
ephemeral ports); vertical tuning of a single generator; distributed/multi-node
generation and coordinating agents; running load from cloud and from Kubernetes;
geographic distribution; aggregating results correctly across nodes (you merge
histograms, you do not average percentiles); cost control at scale.

=== Part IV — Observing and Analyzing

==== Chapter 11. Instrumentation and Observability
*Abstract:* The load test tells you *what* happened on the client. Observability
tells you *why* on the server. You need both, correlated.
Topics: the three signals (metrics, logs, traces) in a performance context;
client-side vs server-side latency and the gap between them; Prometheus and Grafana
for the system under test; OpenTelemetry and distributed tracing to follow a slow
request through services; exporting load-test metrics to the same dashboards; tagging
and correlating a test run with its server-side effects.

==== Chapter 12. Reading the Results Without Fooling Yourself
*Abstract:* The statistics chapter that most books skip and every engineer needs.
Topics: percentiles, revisited, with real data; why "average response time" should
be banned from status reports; coordinated omission, demonstrated and corrected;
comparing two runs with statistical honesty (variance, noise, run-to-run drift);
detecting regressions automatically; visualizing distributions (histograms, CDFs,
latency-over-time, heatmaps); the difference between a real regression and a noisy
environment.

==== Chapter 13. Finding and Fixing Bottlenecks
*Abstract:* You found a slow number. Now what? A systematic hunt from symptom to
root cause.
Topics: the USE method in practice (utilization, saturation, errors per resource);
reading CPU/memory/IO/network saturation; profiling and flame graphs; classic
culprits — connection-pool exhaustion, N+1 queries, lock contention, GC pauses,
chatty services, cold caches; *algorithmic complexity and its abuse* — how a
super-linear code path becomes a denial-of-service, how to find it by scaling an
adversarial input and watching response time bend (worked example using loadr's
payload generator and complexity assertion, plus how to reason about it in any tool);
turning a finding into a fix and proving the fix with a re-test.

=== Part V — Operationalizing Performance

==== Chapter 14. Performance Testing in CI/CD
*Abstract:* Moving from an occasional heroic effort to an automated property of
every change.
Topics: shift-left performance and the smoke-vs-full-run split; running a fast
performance gate on every PR; parameter sweeps and regression detection between
builds; managing flakiness and noisy environments; ephemeral test environments;
storing and trending results over time; failing a build on an SLO breach without
crying wolf.

==== Chapter 15. Continuous and Production Performance
*Abstract:* The test never really ends. Performance validation in and against
production.
Topics: canary and progressive-delivery performance checks; load testing *in*
production safely (traffic shadowing, dark launches, guardrails); chaos and resilience
testing under load; capacity planning and forecasting from test + production data;
validating autoscaling and its lag; log/traffic replay for realistic production-shaped
load.

==== Chapter 16. Capacity, Cost, and Scalability
*Abstract:* Translating test results into infrastructure and money decisions.
Topics: finding the knee — the point where latency degrades non-linearly; headroom
and safety factors; scalability testing (does 2x the nodes give ~2x the throughput?);
Universal Scalability Law as a mental model for diminishing returns and retrograde
scaling; cost-per-request and right-sizing; making the capacity argument to a budget
owner with data.

==== Chapter 17. Building a Performance Culture
*Abstract:* The organizational chapter. Tools and tests fail without ownership,
and this is how they succeed.
Topics: who owns performance (and why "everyone" means "no one"); performance
budgets as a shared, visible contract; dashboards and reporting that non-engineers
trust; runbooks and on-call for performance regressions; SLO governance without
bureaucracy; embedding performance work into planning; anti-patterns (the pre-release
load-test theatre, the ignored dashboard, the vanity benchmark).

=== Appendices

* *Appendix A — Tool quick-reference and cheat sheets.* Side-by-side "how do I do
  X" tables across loadr, k6, JMeter, Gatling, and Locust.
* *Appendix B — Metrics and statistics glossary.* Every term used in the book, with
  the formula and the common misuse.
* *Appendix C — Checklists.* Test-plan checklist, pre-run readiness checklist,
  results-review checklist, and a "is this number a lie?" checklist.

== About the author

Andy Rea is the author of *loadr*, an open-source, single-binary load-testing tool,
and has spent his career building and operating performance-sensitive distributed
systems. He writes the way he'd explain it to a colleague at a whiteboard:
opinionated, concrete, and allergic to hand-waving.
