== Capacity, Cost, and Scalability

The most expensive sentence in infrastructure is "let's double it to be safe."

I watched a retail team say it in October, ahead of their January sale. Nobody
knew what one node could actually handle, so they doubled the Kubernetes node
pool from twelve to twenty-four and held their breath. The sale held. It held,
we worked out much later, because the *old* fleet would have held — peak
traffic reached about 40 percent of the knee of the original twelve nodes. The
doubling bought them nothing except eleven more months of paying for it, at
roughly £70,000, because nobody un-provisions capacity that "got us through
the sale." The fleet had become a superstition.

The opposite failure is cheaper right up until it isn't: the team that runs at
92 percent utilization because the dashboard is green, and discovers during a
marketing push that the last 8 percent of "headroom" was never really there
(<<ch02>> explained why — the queueing penalty grows as ρ/(1−ρ), and the space
between 92 percent and the cliff is measured in seconds, not percent).

Both teams had the same underlying problem. They had load-test results —
plenty of them — but no bridge from the results to the two questions a
business actually asks: *how much can we handle*, and *what does it cost?*
This chapter builds that bridge. Everything in it rests on tests you already
know how to run; the new skill is turning their output into numbers a budget
owner can act on: a capacity figure with evidence behind it, a fleet size
derived by arithmetic instead of anxiety, and a cost per million requests you
can put next to a revenue line.

=== Three questions, three tests

Capacity work decomposes into three questions, and each maps onto a test shape
from <<ch01>>'s taxonomy:

1. *How much can one unit handle?* — a capacity test. Step the load, find the
   knee, and call the knee your unit capacity. Not the maximum; the knee.
2. *Does adding units help, and for how long?* — a scalability test. Measure
   capacity at 1x, 2x, 4x, 8x units and see whether the return is
   proportional, diminishing, or — it happens — negative.
3. *What does a request cost?* — arithmetic on the first two answers plus your
   cloud bill. This is where right-sizing decisions live.

Answer them in that order. A scalability test without a per-unit capacity
figure has nothing to be relative *to*, and a cost model built on "maximum
throughput" instead of "throughput at the knee" prices a regime you must never
operate in.

=== Finding the knee

<<ch02>> gave you the theory: plot latency against offered load and every real
system produces the same curve — flat, flat, slightly up, cliff. The knee is
where latency leaves the flat region, and the knee, not the point of collapse,
is your capacity. <<ch03>> gave you the load shape: step through increasing
levels, giving each level time to settle. What's left is the tedium of running
one plan at five load levels and lining the results up — which is exactly the
tedium worth automating.

Here is a capacity probe against a single application node, using the plan
shapes from <<ch08>>:

[source,yaml]
----
# perf/capacity.yaml — one journey, load level supplied by the sweep
name: capacity-probe
description: step load against one node to find the knee

defaults:
  http:
    base_url: https://api.internal.example.com

scenarios:
  orders:
    executor: constant-vus
    vus: 50                # overridden per combo by the sweep
    duration: 5m
    think_time: { type: uniform, min: 100ms, max: 300ms }
    flow:
      - request:
          name: list_orders
          url: /orders?status=open
          checks: [ { type: status, equals: 200 } ]

thresholds:
  http_req_failed: [ "rate<0.05" ]
----

[source,console]
----
$ loadr sweep perf/capacity.yaml --var vus=10,25,50,100,200
----

`loadr sweep` runs the plan once per value — each combination as a full,
sequential run, so the load levels never contend with each other for generator
resources — and tabulates the results:

[source,text]
----
  combo    p50      p95       p99       error rate  rps
  -------  -------  --------  --------  ----------  --------
  vus=10   18.20ms  24.90ms   31.00ms   0.00%       55.1/s
  vus=25   18.90ms  26.10ms   34.80ms   0.00%       137.4/s
  vus=50   19.10ms  28.40ms   40.20ms   0.00%       270.8/s
  vus=100  24.70ms  61.30ms   112.50ms  0.02%       509.2/s
  vus=200  71.40ms  412.80ms  1.28s     2.31%       541.0/s
----

Read it as two curves sharing an x-axis. The `rps` column is the throughput
curve: it climbs almost linearly to 509/s, then gains only 6 percent more —
541/s — while the load doubles. The latency columns are the queueing curve:
p99 flat through 50 VUs, 3x at 100, 11x at 200. Between the last two rows the
system stopped converting extra demand into extra work and started converting
it into queue depth. That's the knee — somewhere between 100 and 200 VUs —
and the first sweep has bracketed it. Refine with a second, narrower sweep:

[source,console]
----
$ loadr sweep perf/capacity.yaml --var vus=100,125,150,175
----

[source,text]
----
  combo    p50      p95       p99       error rate  rps
  -------  -------  --------  --------  ----------  --------
  vus=100  24.70ms  61.30ms   112.50ms  0.02%       509.2/s
  vus=125  31.20ms  88.60ms   173.40ms  0.05%       527.9/s
  vus=150  44.80ms  169.00ms  402.10ms  0.31%       538.5/s
  vus=175  58.90ms  297.40ms  890.00ms  1.12%       540.2/s
----

Now put your SLO on the table. If this service promises p99 under 400ms
(<<ch04>>), the last row inside the promise is 125 VUs, where the node
delivers just under 530 requests per second — by 150 the p99 has crossed the
line while throughput gained 2 percent. Call it, conservatively: *one node's
capacity is about 520 rps*. That single number, with this table behind it as evidence, is
the foundation everything else in this chapter is built on.

[TIP]
====
Bracket, then refine. Two coarse-then-narrow sweeps of four or five levels
each locate a knee faster and more defensibly than one giant matrix, and each
row is a full settled run rather than a stage inside one long test — stepping
load within a single run lets queue buildup from one level contaminate the
next, which smears exactly the transition you're looking for.
====

Any swept variable is also exported to the run as an environment variable
(`LOADR_SWEEP_<NAME>`), so the same machinery sweeps axes that aren't load at
all — page sizes, payload sizes, cache TTLs — via `${env.*}` interpolation in
the plan. Sweeping `page_size=10,100,1000` against a listing endpoint is a
capacity test of a different dimension, and it catches the super-linear code
paths <<ch13>> showed you before a large customer does.

The concept is tool-neutral even where the automation isn't. In k6 the
equivalent probe is a `ramping-arrival-rate` executor stepping through plateaus
— here as an open-model probe, which is often the better shape for knee-finding
because the injected rate won't politely slow down when the target does
(<<ch03>>):

[source,javascript]
----
export const options = {
  scenarios: {
    steps: {
      executor: 'ramping-arrival-rate',
      startRate: 100, timeUnit: '1s',
      preAllocatedVUs: 600,
      stages: [
        { target: 100, duration: '30s' },  // ramp to the level...
        { target: 100, duration: '5m' },   // ...then hold: each stage ramps
        { target: 200, duration: '30s' },  // linearly to its target, so a
        { target: 200, duration: '5m' },   // plateau is a ramp+hold pair
        // ...one pair per level, up to 500
      ],
    },
  },
};
----

You'll need to slice the results by time window yourself, or script one run
per level — Locust's `LoadTestShape` classes and JMeter's Throughput Shaping
Timer occupy the same niche with the same caveat. However you drive it, the
analysis is identical: find where latency leaves the flat.

[NOTE]
====
In an open-model probe the saturation alarm isn't only latency — it's the
generator failing to inject the schedule. loadr surfaces this as
`dropped_iterations` (threshold it: `dropped_iterations: [ "count<10" ]`);
k6 reports the same metric under the same name. A rising drop count means
you've found the knee even if you weren't looking at the latency table.
====

One warning before you trust any of these numbers: re-read <<ch10>>. A knee
measurement is only as honest as the generator behind it, and a load generator
running near its own ceiling produces a beautiful, wrong knee that is really
*its* knee. Generator CPU below 70 percent, or the run doesn't count.

=== Headroom and safety factors

"One node does 520 rps" is a measurement. "We need eleven nodes" is a
decision, and the distance between them is headroom — the deliberate,
itemized gap between what you provision and what you expect. Itemized is the
key word. "Double it" is a safety factor chosen by vibes; what follows is the
same instinct done with a pencil.

Start from the forecast: say marketing and last year's traffic put the January
peak at *1,800 rps* (<<ch16>> covers where honest forecasts come from). Then
stack the factors, each with a reason attached:

* *Forecast error.* Traffic forecasts miss. If yours have historically been
  within ±25 percent, design for 1,800 × 1.25 = *2,250 rps*. If you don't
  know your forecast error, that itself is the finding.
* *Operating point.* You never plan to run *at* the knee — the knee is where
  bursts stop being absorbed and start being amplified (<<ch02>>'s ρ/(1−ρ)
  again). A sane default is to size so the design load lands at 65–70 percent
  of aggregate knee capacity. At 70 percent: 2,250 / 0.70 ≈ *3,215 rps* of
  knee capacity required.
* *Failure and deploys.* A rolling deploy takes a node out of service; so
  does a hardware failure, and Murphy schedules both for peak hour. Add one
  node beyond whatever the arithmetic demands (N+1), two if a single node is
  a large fraction of the fleet.

So the fleet must offer ~3,215 rps of knee capacity, plus one node. If nodes
added up linearly, that's 3,215 / 520 ≈ 6.2 → seven nodes, plus one, is
eight. But nodes do not add up linearly — which is the next section, and the
place where naive capacity plans quietly go wrong.

=== Does doubling actually double? Scalability testing

The scalability test is the simplest test in this book to describe and one of
the most neglected in practice: measure capacity at several fleet sizes and
see what you actually get. Provision one node, find the knee with the sweep
above. Provision two, repeat. Then four, then eight. Export each run's summary
(`--summary-export`) so the results are comparable artifacts rather than
screenshots.

Here's a real-shaped result for our orders service, capacity defined as
before — max rps with p99 inside the 400ms SLO. (The 1-node row re-measures
last section's knee and gets 510, not 520. That's normal run-to-run variance,
and a reason to keep the raw tables behind every headline number.)

[cols="1,2,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| Nodes | Capacity (rps) | Per node | Efficiency
| 1 | 510 | 510 | 100%
| 2 | 976 | 488 | 96%
| 4 | 1,774 | 444 | 87%
| 8 | 2,873 | 359 | 70%
|===

Doubling never doubles. Two nodes buy you 1.91x, not 2x; eight nodes buy 5.6x.
Every real distributed system does this, and the *shape* of the falloff tells
you why — which is what the next section's model is for. First, though, the
two ways this test lies:

[WARNING]
====
A scalability test measures the scalability of *everything in the path*, not
just the tier you resized. The two classic invalidators:

* *The generator is the ceiling.* If your load rig maxes out at 2,900 rps,
  then 8 nodes, 16 nodes, and 64 nodes will all "measure" 2,900 rps and
  you'll conclude the system stopped scaling. Scale the generator fleet with
  the target (<<ch10>>) and re-verify its headroom at every fleet size.
* *A dependency you didn't scale.* If all N app nodes share one database, one
  cache, one downstream rate limit, the test is measuring *that*. This isn't
  necessarily invalid — production shares the same database! — but be precise
  about what the result describes: the scalability of the system as deployed,
  not of the application tier in the abstract.
====

=== The Universal Scalability Law, as a mental model

You can go a long way with the table above and a ruler. But there's a compact
model that explains the falloff, fits on an index card, and — used carefully —
predicts where scaling stops paying. Neil Gunther's Universal Scalability Law
says the capacity of N units is:

[source,text]
----
C(N) = N / (1 + σ(N − 1) + κ·N(N − 1))
----

Two coefficients, two physical stories:

* *σ (contention)* is the fraction of work that is serialized — the part of
  every request that queues for something shared: a lock, a leader, a
  connection pool, a single writer. Contention alone gives you Amdahl's Law:
  returns diminish toward a ceiling of 1/σ, but adding units never *hurts*.
* *κ (crosstalk, or coherency)* is the cost of units talking to each other to
  stay consistent — cache invalidation chatter, lock convoys across nodes,
  gossip, distributed transactions. It grows with N², because every unit
  pairs with every other. Crosstalk is what makes returns go *retrograde*:
  past a certain N, adding a node reduces total throughput.

Fit the model to the measured table (least squares on four points — a
spreadsheet will do, and R and Python fits are one search away) and our orders
service comes out at roughly σ ≈ 0.04 and κ ≈ 0.0025. Four percent of each
request serialized — that was the shared Postgres, it usually is — and a
small-looking crosstalk term from cross-node cache invalidation. Small-looking
until you evaluate the curve: the model peaks around *N ≈ 20 nodes at ~3,800
rps*, and at 32 nodes total throughput is *lower* than at 20. There is no
number of nodes at which this system serves 5,000 rps. That sentence is worth
the whole exercise: it converts "we can always add more nodes" from an
assumption into a testable, and here false, claim.

Now the capacity plan from the previous section can be finished honestly. We
needed ~3,215 rps of knee capacity. On the naive linear model that's seven
nodes; on the fitted curve, C(9) ≈ 6.0 → 3,060 rps and C(10) ≈ 6.3 → 3,215
rps. *Ten nodes, plus one for N+1: eleven.* Not the twelve-doubled-to-24 of
the superstition fleet, and not the naive eight either — and every node in the
figure has a line of arithmetic attached that a reviewer can check.

The model also reframes the engineering conversation. σ and κ are not facts of
nature; they're properties of the architecture, and <<ch13>>'s bottleneck hunt
is how you attack them. Halve the crosstalk (coarser cache invalidation,
sharding the hot table) and the peak moves from 20 nodes to 28 and the ceiling
rises by a quarter — which may be worth more than any number of additional
nodes, and costs less.

[WARNING]
====
The USL is a mental model with a fit, not an oracle. Fitted on measurements at
1–8 nodes, treat its predictions at 20 as a hypothesis to test before you bet
a launch on them, and its predictions at 200 as fiction. Systems also change
regime — a new bottleneck surfaces, a cache stops fitting in memory — and the
coefficients change with them. Re-fit whenever the architecture does.
====

=== Cost per request and right-sizing

Capacity numbers become business numbers the moment you divide by them. The
unit that travels best is *cost per million requests*, and the arithmetic is
one line: an instance costing $0.17/hour with a knee of 520 rps can serve
520 × 3,600 ≈ 1.87M requests per hour, so its floor cost is *$0.091 per
million requests*. Floor, because that's the price at the knee — at the 70
percent operating point you actually run at, the real figure is $0.13/M. Use
the operating-point number in budgets and the knee number in comparisons.

Comparisons are where this gets useful, because the knee is not proportional
to instance size and your cloud provider's pricing is. Run the same sweep on
each candidate instance type and price the result:

[cols="2,1,1,2,2",options="header"]
|===
| Instance | $/hr | Knee (rps) | $/M at knee | $/M at 70%
| c6i.large (2 vCPU) | $0.085 | 240 | $0.098 | $0.140
| c6i.xlarge (4 vCPU) | $0.170 | 520 | $0.091 | $0.130
| c6i.2xlarge (8 vCPU) | $0.340 | 950 | $0.099 | $0.142
|===

The xlarge is the cheapest per request for this workload — the large pays
per-instance overhead on too few cores, and the 2xlarge hits an internal
contention wall (σ operates inside a box exactly as it does across a fleet)
before it earns its price. That's a right-sizing decision made with an
afternoon of sweeps, and it's workload-specific: the same table for a
memory-bound service ranks differently, which is why you measure instead of
adopting someone else's conclusion.

Two refinements worth carrying:

* *Price the SLO, not just the request.* <<ch04>> argued that "meets the SLO
  at a cost we can afford" is the real requirement. A proposal that improves
  p99 by 40ms for an extra $3,100/month is now a purchasable object — and so
  is its rejection.
* *Idle capacity is the silent line item.* Cost per request assumes the
  requests show up. A fleet sized for January running flat through June has a
  real cost per request several times the table above. This is the honest
  argument for autoscaling — and the reason autoscaling configs deserve load
  tests of their own (<<ch16>>): an autoscaler that reacts in four minutes to
  a spike that peaks in one is idle capacity you paid for and an outage you
  got anyway.

=== Making the case to a budget owner

Everything so far produces numbers. The last step is producing a *decision*,
and decisions are made by people who will not read your sweep tables. Fifteen
years of capacity meetings have taught me that the argument that works fits on
one page and has five parts:

1. *What we can handle today*, as a single number with a date: "~2,870 rps at
   SLO, measured on the 14th" — with the sweep matrix and summary exports
   archived behind the link, not pasted into the page.
2. *What's coming*, with its source named: "forecast peak 1,800 rps ±25%,
   from last January's curve plus marketing's uplift."
3. *The proposal, in money*: "grow from 8 to 11 nodes for the sale window:
   +$370/month for the quarter."
4. *The cost of being wrong in each direction.* Under-provisioning: last
   year's checkout brownout at peak cost roughly £40k/hour in abandoned
   carts. Over-provisioning: the standing cost of nodes we can name and
   de-provision in February — and a calendar entry to do it.
5. *The cheaper alternative, if one exists*: "fixing the cache-invalidation
   crosstalk (two engineer-weeks) raises the scaling ceiling ~30% and removes
   two nodes from every future plan."

Notice what this structure does: it never asks the budget owner to trust your
engineering. Every claim has a measurement behind it, both failure modes are
priced instead of only the scary one, and the request comes with its own
expiry date. That last item is what separates a capacity plan from a ratchet —
fleets that only ever grow are how the superstition fleet from the opening
got built.

The evidence trail is the part to automate, because a capacity figure decays
— every deploy moves the knee a little, and one bad query moves it a lot. Put
the sweep on a schedule (<<ch15>> wires this properly): a nightly or weekly
job runs the matrix, publishes the table to the build summary, and archives
the per-combo exports. When a knee moves, you find out from a diff, not from
January:

[source,console]
----
$ loadr compare last-week/sweep-vus-100.json today/sweep-vus-100.json
----

[source,text]
----
  metric             field       baseline  current   delta     delta %
  -----------------  ----------  --------  --------  --------  --------  -
  http_req_duration  p95         61.30ms   131.20ms  +69.90ms  ▲ 114.0%  ✗
  http_req_duration  p99         112.50ms  241.80ms  +129.3ms  ▲ 114.9%  ✗
  http_reqs          per_second  509.2/s   448.7/s   -60.5/s   ▼ 11.9%

✗ 2 regression(s) beyond tolerance
----

That's a capacity regression caught while it's a code review comment instead
of a postmortem: same load level, p99 doubled, throughput down 12 percent —
the knee just moved left, and `git log` for the week is now a short suspect
list for <<ch13>>'s hunt. A team that can produce this table on demand never
again answers "can we handle the sale?" with a shrug or a doubled fleet. They
answer it with a number, its date, and its evidence — which is the entire
difference between capacity planning and capacity guessing. What remains, in
<<ch18>>, is making sure somebody owns it.

.Key takeaways
****
* Capacity work is three measurements in order: the *knee of one unit*, the
  *return on adding units*, and the *cost per request* — each built on the
  one before.
* *The knee is your capacity*, not the maximum. Bracket it with a coarse
  sweep, refine with a narrow one, and read throughput and tail latency as
  two curves on one axis: capacity ends where extra load buys queue depth
  instead of throughput.
* *Headroom is itemized, not vibes*: forecast error, a 65–70 percent
  operating point below the knee, and N+1 for failures and deploys. "Double
  it to be safe" is a budget line with no argument attached.
* *Doubling never doubles.* Measure capacity at 1x/2x/4x/8x, and make sure
  the ceiling you find isn't your load generator or an unscaled dependency.
* The *Universal Scalability Law* turns the falloff into two named causes —
  contention (σ) and crosstalk (κ) — and predicts where adding nodes stops
  paying and starts hurting. Fit it, use it as a hypothesis, never as an
  oracle far beyond your data.
* *Cost per million requests at the operating point* is the number that
  travels: it right-sizes instance types, prices SLO improvements, and
  exposes idle capacity.
* Budget owners buy *one page*: today's capacity with a date, the forecast
  with a source, the proposal in money, both failure modes priced, and the
  cheaper architectural alternative. Automate the evidence with scheduled
  sweeps and run-over-run comparison so the knee can't move without you
  noticing.
****
