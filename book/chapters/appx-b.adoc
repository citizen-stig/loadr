== Metrics and Statistics Glossary

Performance testing has a small vocabulary with sharp edges. Most arguments about
a test result turn out to be two people using the same word for different things
— "latency" for two different intervals, "concurrency" for two different models.
This glossary is the book's contract: every entry gives the definition as used in
these pages, the formula where one exists, and the misuse that most often bites.
Terms are grouped by concept rather than alphabetized, since half of them define
each other.

=== Time and its distribution

Latency, response time, service time::
Three intervals, routinely collapsed into one word. *Service time* is the time a
request spends actually being worked on. *Latency*, in the strict queueing-theory
sense, is the time spent _waiting_ to be worked on. *Response time* is the sum —
what a client experiences, usually including network transit. Most tools
(including every load tester in this book) say "latency" and mean client-observed
response time; this book follows that convention except in <<ch13>>, where the
decomposition matters.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ comparing a server-side service time to a client-observed
response time and calling the difference a regression. Different intervals,
different clocks, different truths.

Percentile::
The value below which a given fraction of observations fall. "p99 = 300ms" means
99 percent of measured requests completed in 300ms or less — and one in a hundred
took longer, possibly much longer. Percentiles are how you talk about the tail,
which <<ch01>> argues is what users actually experience.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ averaging percentiles — across nodes, workers, or time
windows. The average of ten p99s is not the p99 of the combined traffic, and can
be wildly wrong. Merge the underlying histograms, then read the percentile off
the merged distribution.

Histogram::
Bucketed counts of observations: how many requests landed between 10 and 20ms,
between 20 and 30ms, and so on. The histogram _is_ the result of a load test;
every summary statistic is a lossy projection of it. Unlike percentiles,
histograms merge correctly — which is why serious tools ship them between
workers instead of shipping pre-computed summaries.

HDR histogram::
A High Dynamic Range histogram: a histogram whose bucket widths scale so that
every recorded value keeps a fixed relative precision (typically two or three
significant figures) across a range from microseconds to hours, in a few hundred
kilobytes of fixed memory. Because the error bound is known and the structure
merges losslessly, HDR histograms are the standard way to aggregate latency
across distributed load generators — loadr records every request into one and
merges per-worker histograms at the end of a run, which is how a distributed
test can report a single honest p99.9.

Coordinated omission::
The measurement bug (named by Gil Tene) where the load generator itself slows
down when the system under test slows down — a virtual user stuck waiting on a
5-second response doesn't send the four requests it "should" have sent during
those seconds, so the slow period is systematically under-sampled and the
recorded percentiles are flattered exactly when the system is at its worst.
The structural fix is an open, arrival-rate model that keeps sending on schedule
regardless of responses; the diagnostic is watching for dropped or delayed
iterations. <<ch12>> treats this at length.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ taking closed-model (fixed-VU) percentiles at face value near
saturation. They are lower bounds on the pain, not measurements of it.

=== Demand and flow

Throughput::
Completed work per unit time — requests per second, messages per second.
Distinguish it from *offered load*, the rate at which work arrives. Under light
load the two match; at saturation, throughput plateaus while offered load keeps
climbing and the difference piles up in queues.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ reading flat-or-rising throughput as health. A system can
hold its req/s steady while its p99 goes from 80ms to 8 seconds.

Arrival rate::
The rate at which new requests _start_, independent of how fast previous ones
finish — the defining knob of an open workload model, and the closest match to
how real internet traffic behaves (your users do not coordinate with each other).
In loadr and k6 this is the `constant-arrival-rate` / `ramping-arrival-rate`
family of executors, introduced in <<ch01>> and dissected in <<ch03>>.

Concurrency::
The number of requests in flight at an instant. In an open model concurrency is
an _output_, not an input — it emerges from arrival rate and response time via
Little's Law. When response time doubles, so does concurrency, which is how slow
systems eat their own connection pools.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ using "concurrency," "VUs," and "users" interchangeably. They
are three different numbers.

Virtual users (VUs)::
A load generator's worker loop pretending to be one user: send, wait for the
response, think, repeat. A fixed VU count defines a *closed* model — total
in-flight work is capped, and the request rate floats with the system's response
time.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ "we tested with 500 VUs" as a statement of load. It isn't
one. 500 VUs against a 50ms system is 10,000 req/s; against a 2-second system it
is 250 req/s. The load adapts to the victim — see coordinated omission.

Little's Law::
For any system in steady state:
+
[source,text]
----
L = λ × W
concurrency = throughput × response time
----
+
No distributional assumptions, no exceptions. Use it to size VU pools (how many
workers do I need to sustain 200 req/s at 300ms? 200 × 0.3 = 60), and to
sanity-check results — if a report's three numbers don't satisfy the identity,
the report is wrong.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ applying it during ramps or spikes. It holds in steady state
only; average over a stable window.

=== Resources and health

Utilization::
The fraction of time a resource is busy (or of its capacity in use). Queueing
theory's inconvenient gift: waiting time grows roughly with ρ/(1−ρ) as
utilization ρ approaches 1, so the step from 80 to 90 percent busy roughly
_doubles_ queueing delay.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ reading 70 percent CPU as "30 percent headroom." Headroom is
measured in latency at the next load step, not in idle percentage.

Saturation::
The degree to which a resource has more work than it can service — queue depth,
run-queue length, threads waiting. Utilization says "busy"; saturation says
"backed up," and it is saturation that turns into latency.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ equating saturation with 100 percent average utilization. A
resource can saturate in bursts while averaging 40 percent busy; the tail
latency shows it, the utilization graph hides it.

Error rate::
The fraction (or absolute rate) of failed requests — where "failed" must be
defined explicitly: transport errors, HTTP 5xx, timeouts, and failed content
checks are all different failure classes with different meanings.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ two, both nasty. Percentages hide collapse — "2 percent
errors" of a throughput that has fallen 90 percent is not a small problem. And
fast failures flatter latency: a system that starts rejecting requests in 2ms
will show a _better_ p99 as it dies. Always read error rate and latency
together, and only trust latency percentiles computed over successful requests.

USE method::
Brendan Gregg's checklist for _resources_: for every resource (CPU, memory,
disk, network, locks), check *U*tilization, *S*aturation, *E*rrors. The
systematic sweep behind the bottleneck hunt in <<ch13>>.

RED method::
The companion checklist for _services_: for every request-driven service, watch
*R*ate, *E*rrors, *D*uration. RED describes what your users feel; USE explains
why. <<ch11>> wires both into a load test.

Knee::
The point on the load-versus-latency curve where latency stops creeping and
starts climbing — the beginning of queueing dominance. The knee, not the
breakpoint, is your usable capacity; running production near the knee means any
traffic blip lands on the steep part of the curve. Capacity tests (<<ch16>>)
exist to find it: step the arrival rate up in stages and watch for the bend.

=== Objectives and contracts

SLI (service level indicator)::
A measurement chosen to stand for user experience — typically a ratio of good
events to valid events: the fraction of requests faster than 300ms, the fraction
returning a correct, non-error response. You can't set a target until you've
picked the indicator, which is why <<ch04>> spends its first half here.

SLO (service level objective)::
A target on an SLI over a window: "99 percent of checkout requests complete
under 300ms, measured over 30 days." The load-test form of an SLO is a
machine-checkable gate, which is what makes a load test a _test_:
+
[source,yaml]
----
thresholds:
  http_req_duration:
    - "slo(99%) < 300ms"    # loadr exits non-zero if breached
  http_req_failed:
    - "rate<0.001"          # 99.9% availability
----
+
(The `slo(99%)` spelling is loadr's; k6 writes the same gate as `p(99)<300`, and
loadr accepts that form too. JMeter and Gatling have equivalent assertions. The
tool doesn't matter; the existence of the gate does.)

SLA (service level agreement)::
An SLO with a contract and consequences attached — usually money, owed to a
customer when the target is missed.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ using SLA and SLO interchangeably. Your external SLA should
always be _looser_ than your internal SLO, so your team notices and reacts long
before your customers can invoice you.

Error budget::
The complement of the SLO: a 99.9 percent objective grants a budget of 0.1
percent bad events per window — roughly 43 minutes per month. The budget is
meant to be _spent_: on deploys, experiments, chaos drills, and load tests
against production (<<ch15>>). A team that never touches its budget is
over-cautious; a team that burns it in week one stops shipping.

Apdex::
The Application Performance Index: pick a threshold T, count requests under T as
satisfied, between T and 4T as tolerating, beyond 4T as frustrated, then:
+
[source,text]
----
Apdex = (satisfied + tolerating/2) / total
----
+
One number between 0 and 1, easy to put on an executive dashboard.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ treating it as sufficient. Apdex compresses an entire
distribution into one figure with an arbitrary T; an Apdex of 0.94 says nothing
about whether your worst 1 percent waited 2 seconds or 2 minutes. Fine as a
trend line, never as the only gate.

=== Scaling

Universal Scalability Law (USL)::
Neil Gunther's model for how throughput responds to added resources N:
+
[source,text]
----
C(N) = N / (1 + σ(N−1) + κ·N(N−1))
----
+
σ is *contention* (serialized work — queues for a shared lock or resource) and κ
is *coherency* (crosstalk — the cost of keeping N parties consistent). σ alone
caps your speedup (Amdahl's Law is the κ=0 special case); any κ > 0 means
throughput _peaks and then declines_ as you add nodes — the cluster where 12
instances serve less traffic than 8. Fit it to measured throughput at several
scales to predict the peak; <<ch16>> walks through the fit.
+
_Misuse to avoid:_ fitting σ and κ to two or three data points and extrapolating
far beyond the measured range. The USL interpolates honestly and extrapolates
optimistically; measure at least four or five scale points before trusting the
curve.
