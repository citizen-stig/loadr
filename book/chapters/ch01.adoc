== Why Performance Is a Feature

In 2006, Google ran an experiment that has been quoted so many times it's become
a kind of folklore. They increased the number of search results per page from ten
to thirty. More results, one round trip — surely a better experience. Traffic and
revenue from that segment dropped by around 20 percent. The culprit, once they
dug in, was half a second: the thirty-result page took roughly 500 milliseconds
longer to generate, and that half second was enough to measurably change how much
people searched.

Around the same time, Amazon reported that every additional 100 milliseconds of
latency cost them roughly 1 percent in sales. Akamai and others have since put
similar numbers on retail conversion, bounce rate, and engagement. The exact
figures vary by study, industry, and decade, and you should be suspicious of
anyone who quotes them to three significant figures. But the shape of the finding
is remarkably stable and worth internalizing before we write a single line of a
test:

[quote]
____
Speed is not a technical nicety that lives downstream of the "real" features.
Speed *is* a feature. For a lot of products, it is one of the most valuable
features you have, and it is the one users never explicitly ask for and always
punish you for missing.
____

This chapter makes the case for why performance deserves the same rigor,
tooling, and continuous attention as correctness — and then draws the map for the
rest of the book. If you already believe performance matters and just want to
learn to test it well, skim to <<taxonomy>>, where we lay out the kinds of tests
and what each one is actually for. But I'd encourage you to read the whole thing,
because the mistakes in later chapters almost always trace back to a fuzzy answer
to the question this chapter asks: _what are we even trying to protect, and for
whom?_

=== The three cases for caring

There are three distinct arguments for taking performance seriously. They're
often mushed together, but they persuade different people and they have different
consequences for how you test.

==== The business case

The first is the one we opened with: performance moves money. Slower pages
convert worse, retain worse, and rank worse (search engines have used speed as a
ranking signal for years). If your product makes money as a function of user
engagement, latency is a lever on revenue as directly as price or availability.

The trap in the business case is quoting someone else's numbers as if they were
yours. Google's 20 percent and Amazon's 1-percent-per-100ms are *their* numbers,
for *their* products, in *their* era. The discipline is to measure your own:
run the experiment, correlate your own latency with your own conversion or
engagement, and put a local price on a millisecond. Once you can say "100ms at
p95 checkout is worth roughly £X per month to us," performance work stops
competing with features for priority and starts being obviously fundable.

==== The human case

The second argument is about perception, and it's older than the web. Human
beings have fairly consistent thresholds for how delay feels, and they've been
studied since at least the 1960s:

* *Under ~100ms* feels instantaneous. The user perceives a direct causal link
  between their action and the system's response — they feel like they're
  manipulating the thing directly.
* *Around 1 second* is the limit for keeping a user's flow of thought
  uninterrupted. They notice the delay but stay focused. No feedback is strictly
  required yet, though it helps.
* *Around 10 seconds* is roughly the limit of attention. Past that, people
  mentally context-switch — they'll go check another tab, and you've lost them.
  If an operation will take longer, you owe them a progress indicator and
  ideally something to do meanwhile.

These thresholds are why a 200ms API call that feels fine in isolation can add up
to a page that feels sluggish, and why perceived performance (does something
happen *now*?) can matter as much as raw throughput. We'll come back to this when
we talk about what to measure: a p50 that sits comfortably under 100ms tells you
nothing about the user whose p99 request took four seconds.

==== The reliability case

The third argument is the one engineers underrate, and it's the reason a
performance book and a reliability book keep bumping into each other:
*slow is the on-ramp to down.*

A system that's merely slow rarely stays merely slow under load. Requests pile
up. Each one holds a connection, a thread, a chunk of memory a little longer than
it should. Queues grow. Timeouts fire, and timeouts trigger retries, and retries
add load, and now you have a retry storm feeding a system that was already
struggling — a textbook congestive collapse. The failure mode that takes down
production is very often not "the code was wrong" but "the code was fine and just
too slow under real load, and everything downstream reacted to the slowness by
making it worse."

This is why performance testing is not a subspecies of QA that you can safely
defer. A latency regression is a latent outage. The whole back half of this book
— capacity, autoscaling, chaos under load, production guardrails — is really
about finding that latent outage before your users do.

=== The tyranny of the average, and the tail that bites

Here is the single most important idea in this chapter, and arguably in the book.
We'll spend all of Chapter 2 making it precise and Chapter 12 making it rigorous,
but you need the intuition now because it reframes everything.

*Users don't experience your average. They experience your tail.*

Imagine an API that responds in 50ms almost all the time, but 1 percent of
requests take 3 seconds because they miss a cache and hit a cold, contended code
path. The average response time is about 80ms — a number that would sail through
most people's idea of "fast." But consider a single page that makes 100 calls to
this API to render (not unusual in a microservice fan-out). The chance that *at
least one* of those 100 calls lands in the slow 1 percent is:

[source,text]
----
1 - (0.99)^100 ≈ 0.63
----

Roughly *63 percent* of page loads will contain at least one 3-second call. Your
"80ms average" service just made two out of every three page loads feel broken.
This is the arithmetic of tail latency in a fan-out architecture, and it is why
seasoned performance engineers develop an almost physical aversion to the word
"average."

[WARNING]
====
If a status report, a dashboard, or a load-test summary shows you a single
"average response time" and nothing else, treat it as a red flag, not a result.
It is hiding exactly the users you most need to know about. Throughout this book
we'll insist on distributions and high percentiles (p95, p99, and often p99.9),
and in <<ch12>> we'll show how even those can be quietly falsified by a bug in
your load generator called _coordinated omission_.
====

=== Where performance testing sits

It helps to be precise about what performance testing is and isn't, because it
lives next to several neighbors it's easy to confuse it with.

*Functional testing* asks "does it produce the right answer?" Performance testing
assumes the answer is right and asks "does it produce the right answer _fast
enough, under enough concurrent demand, for long enough_?" A system can be
perfectly correct and completely unfit for production because it's too slow. Your
unit tests will be a cheerful, uninformative green the entire time.

*Profiling and observability* ask "_why_ is it behaving this way?" — they explain
a system's internal resource use. Performance testing is what *creates the
conditions* worth observing. You generate representative load (the testing part),
and you use observability to understand what happened inside (the analysis part).
The two are partners, and <<ch11>> is entirely about wiring them together. A load
test without server-side observability tells you *that* something is slow;
observability without load tells you about a system nobody is currently
stressing. You want both, correlated.

*Benchmarking* usually means measuring a narrow, isolated operation — how many
JSON documents per second can this parser handle? It's useful and we'll use
micro-benchmarks in places, but a benchmark is not a load test. Real systems fail
in the interactions: the connection pool that's fine for the parser but starves
under a realistic request mix, the cache that helps in the benchmark and hurts
under the real access pattern.

[[taxonomy]]
=== A taxonomy of performance tests

"Performance testing" is an umbrella. Underneath it are several distinct kinds of
test, each shaped to answer a different question. Confusing them — running one and
interpreting it as another — is one of the most common and expensive mistakes in
the field. Here's the map; we'll design and run each of these in later chapters.

[cols="1,3,2",options="header"]
|===
| Test type | The question it answers | Typical shape
| *Load test* | Does the system meet its SLOs at *expected* peak load? | Ramp to target load, hold, observe
| *Stress test* | Where and how does it *break* when pushed past expected load? | Ramp past peak until it degrades or fails
| *Spike test* | How does it handle a *sudden* jump in traffic (and recover after)? | Idle → instant surge → back to idle
| *Soak / endurance test* | Does it stay healthy over *time* — leaks, fragmentation, drift? | Moderate load held for hours or days
| *Capacity test* | *How much* can it handle before violating SLOs, and where's the knee? | Step up load in stages, find the limit
| *Breakpoint test* | What is the absolute *maximum* before hard failure? | Increase until it falls over
| *Scalability test* | Does adding resources give *proportional* return? | Measure throughput at 1x, 2x, 4x nodes
|===

Notice that these differ along two axes: the *shape of the load over time*
(steady, ramping, spiking, sustained) and the *question about the result* (does it
meet a target? where does it break? does it hold up? does it scale?). Much of the
craft in Part II is choosing the right combination for the question you actually
have — and resisting the very common urge to run a single "load test" and pretend
it answered all of these at once. It didn't.

[NOTE]
====
People use these names loosely and inconsistently in the wild. Someone will say
"we ran a stress test" and mean a load test, or "soak" and mean an hour. Don't be
pedantic about the vocabulary with colleagues, but *be ruthless in your own head*
about which question you're answering. A soak test and a spike test can use the
same tool and the same scenario file and tell you completely unrelated things.
====

=== A first taste

We'll build real tests carefully starting in <<ch08>>, but it's worth seeing the
end state now so the abstractions have something to hang on. Here is a minimal
load test that ramps to 200 requests per second against an API, holds for two
minutes, and asserts that 99 percent of responses come back under 300ms. This is
*loadr*:

[source,yaml]
----
# smoke.yaml — ramp to 200 rps, hold 2m, fail if p99 > 300ms
scenario:
  http:
    - get: https://api.example.com/products
      check:
        status: 200
load:
  arrival_rate:            # note: arrival-rate, an *open* model (Chapter 3)
    - target: 200          # requests per second, not concurrent users
      ramp: 30s
    - target: 200
      hold: 2m
slo:
  http_req_duration:
    p99: 300ms             # the pass/fail gate — a machine can check this
----

[source,console]
----
$ loadr run smoke.yaml
----

And the same intent in *k6*, so you can see the concept survives the change of
tool (the model is identical; only the spelling differs):

[source,javascript]
----
import http from 'k6/http';
import { check } from 'k6';

export const options = {
  scenarios: {
    ramp: {
      executor: 'ramping-arrival-rate',   // open model, like above
      startRate: 0, timeUnit: '1s',
      preAllocatedVUs: 200,
      stages: [
        { target: 200, duration: '30s' },
        { target: 200, duration: '2m' },
      ],
    },
  },
  thresholds: { http_req_duration: ['p(99)<300'] },  // the same gate
};

export default function () {
  const res = http.get('https://api.example.com/products');
  check(res, { 'status is 200': (r) => r.status === 200 });
}
----

Two things to notice, both of which we'll spend real time on later. First, both
tests specify load as an *arrival rate* — 200 requests per second — not as a
number of concurrent users. That choice (the "open model") is not cosmetic; it
fundamentally changes what the test measures, and getting it wrong is one of the
subtlest ways to run a test that lies. <<ch03>> is largely about this. Second,
both tests carry an explicit, machine-checkable success criterion — `p99 <
300ms`. A load test without a pass/fail gate isn't a test; it's a number
generator. <<ch04>> is about writing gates that fail loudly and mean something.

=== What the rest of this book does

Here's the arc, so you know where we're going.

*Part I (Foundations)* — this chapter, plus <<ch02>>, which gives you the
vocabulary and the five statistical ideas — percentiles, Little's Law,
coordinated omission, saturation, and the SLI/SLO/error-budget frame — that
everything after depends on.

*Part II (Designing the Test)* — how to make a test reflect reality: modeling the
workload (<<ch03>>), turning "fast enough" into checkable objectives (<<ch04>>),
getting the test data and environment right (<<ch05>>), and composing believable
scenarios (<<ch06>>). This is where most tests are won or lost, before a single
request is sent.

*Part III (Executing Tests)* — an honest survey of the tooling landscape
(<<ch07>>), your first real end-to-end test (<<ch08>>), testing everything that
isn't plain HTTP (<<ch09>>), and generating load at a scale where the load
generator itself doesn't become the bottleneck (<<ch10>>).

*Part IV (Observing and Analyzing)* — instrumenting the system under test so you
can see *why* (<<ch11>>), reading results without fooling yourself (<<ch12>>,
the statistics chapter I'd send you to first), and the systematic hunt from a
slow number to its root cause (<<ch13>>), including how a super-linear code path
quietly becomes a denial-of-service.

*Part V (Operationalizing)* — making performance continuous: gating it in CI/CD
(<<ch15>>), validating it in and against production (<<ch16>>), turning results
into capacity and cost decisions (<<ch17>>), and building the organizational
habits that keep all of it alive (<<ch18>>).

By the end you should be able to walk into a room, put a distribution on the
screen, and say — with evidence you'd stake your reputation on — exactly how the
system behaves under load, where it breaks, and what it'll cost to make it
better. That's the job. Let's learn to do it honestly.

.Key takeaways
****
* Performance is a product feature with a measurable price; the discipline is to
  measure *your* price, not to quote someone else's numbers.
* Users experience your *tail*, not your average — and in a fan-out
  architecture, a rare slow request becomes a common slow *page*.
* "Slow" is the on-ramp to "down": latency regressions are latent outages.
* Performance testing *creates* the conditions; observability *explains* them.
  You need both, correlated.
* There isn't one "performance test." There's a family of them — load, stress,
  spike, soak, capacity, breakpoint, scalability — each answering a different
  question. Know which one you're running.
****

[.fieldcard]
.Field Card — Framing a Performance Test
****
Before you generate a single request, answer two things: which test is this, and what number makes it fail?

Picking the test type:: match the question to the shape — SLOs at peak = *load*; where it breaks = *stress*; sudden surge then recovery = *spike*; leaks over hours = *soak*; the SLO knee = *capacity*. Running one and reading it as another is the classic expensive mistake.
Reporting a result:: never a lone average — it hides the users you most need to see. Report the distribution and p95/p99/p99.9.
Modeling the load:: use an *open* model — arrival rate in requests/sec, not concurrent users. VUs only cap concurrency; they don't set demand.
Making it a test:: attach a machine-checkable gate. No pass/fail gate and it's a number generator, not a test.

[source,yaml]
----
scenarios:
  steady:
    executor: constant-arrival-rate
    rate: 200            # req/s — open model, not VUs
    time_unit: 1s
    duration: 2m
    pre_allocated_vus: 50
    max_vus: 200
thresholds:
  http_req_duration: [ "p(99)<300" ]   # the gate
----

[source,console]
----
$ loadr run smoke.yaml
----

*Remember:* perception cliffs — <100ms feels instant, ~1s breaks flow, ~10s loses them. Fan-out tail: 1 − 0.99^100 ≈ 63% of pages hit that slow 1%. Slow is the on-ramp to down.
****
