== Reading the Results Without Fooling Yourself

Run the same load test twice against a system nobody touched, and the two p95s
will not match. On a quiet, dedicated rig they might differ by 2 percent. On a
shared cloud staging environment I have watched back-to-back runs of an
identical plan disagree by 15 percent — and I have watched a team spend two
days hunting the "regression" between them. There was no regression. There
were two honest measurements of a noisy world, and a reader who didn't know
how much noise to expect.

That's this chapter's subject. Everything before this point in the book was
about generating numbers worth reading; this chapter is about reading them
without fooling yourself. It's the statistics chapter most performance books
skip, and it needs almost no mathematics — just a handful of habits: distrust
of averages, respect for sample sizes, one famous measurement bug and its fix,
four pictures worth drawing, and a disciplined procedure for the most common
question in the field: _is this a real regression, or did the environment
sneeze?_

<<ch02>> introduced percentiles and coordinated omission as vocabulary. Here
we make them rigorous, with real data.

=== Ban the average from your status reports

Take one minute of traffic against a checkout endpoint at 100 requests per
second: 6,000 samples. The endpoint has two code paths — a cache hit that
returns in about 41ms, and a cache miss that goes to the pricing service and
takes about 4 seconds. Misses are 2 percent of traffic. Here is the same
dataset summarized four ways:

[cols="1,1,3",options="header"]
|===
| Statistic | Value | What it describes
| mean | ~120ms | a request that *cannot occur* — nothing took between 60ms and 3.8s
| p50 | 41ms | the typical cache hit
| p95 | 45ms | still a cache hit; the tail hasn't started yet
| p99 | ~4.1s | the cache miss — the user who leaves
|===

The average, 120ms, sits in a valley of the distribution where zero requests
actually landed. It is not a poor estimate of the user experience; it is a
precise description of a user who does not exist. Worse, it fails to move for
the right reasons: make the misses twice as frequent and half as slow and the
mean stays at 120ms — though twice as many customers are now having a bad
day. The mean cannot tell those two worlds apart. Your users can.

This is why the instruction in the section title is literal. When a status
report says "average response time: 120ms," it is compressing a bimodal
distribution — two distinct system behaviors — into one number that describes
neither, and it is hiding the 2 percent of customers having a four-second day.
Report a distribution or, at minimum, p50 / p95 / p99 plus the error rate.
Anyone who wants "one number" can have p95; at least p95 is a promise about
real requests.

[WARNING]
====
Averages don't just blur — they compose misleadingly. An "average latency"
line on a dashboard is usually the mean of per-interval means, weighted by
whatever the traffic happened to be. It will smooth directly over a
thirty-second stall that took every user in it to timeout. The stall is right
there in the p99-over-time chart, and completely invisible in the average.
====

=== Percentiles, revisited with real data

Percentiles fix the averaging problem, but they come with two sharp edges:
they need samples, and they don't compose.

==== A percentile is only as good as its sample count

The p99 of a run is estimated from the slowest 1 percent of samples. Run a
30-second smoke test at 20 requests per second and you have 600 samples —
your p99 is riding on the *six* slowest requests, and your "p99.9" is
literally your single slowest request wearing a lab coat. One unlucky DNS
lookup and the number swings 400ms; one lucky run and your gate passes a
build it shouldn't.

My working rule: I want at least 100 samples *beyond* the percentile before I
treat it as a measurement rather than an anecdote. That means:

* *p95* — at least ~2,000 samples,
* *p99* — at least ~10,000 samples,
* *p99.9* — at least ~100,000 samples.

At 100 rps, that's 20 seconds, 100 seconds, and about 17 minutes of steady
load respectively. This is one of the quiet reasons soak-length runs exist,
and it's why a CI smoke test (<<ch15>>) should gate on p95, not p99.9: not
because the deep tail doesn't matter, but because a two-minute run doesn't
contain enough information to measure it. Gating on a percentile your run
can't support doesn't make the gate stricter. It makes it random.

==== Percentiles don't compose

Here is a trap that catches nearly everyone running distributed load
(<<ch10>>). Two load agents each report a p95. Agent A says 100ms; agent B,
on a worse network path, says 500ms. What's the overall p95?

You cannot know. Not "it's hard to compute" — the information isn't there.
The answer depends on the full shape of both distributions, and it is almost
never the average (300ms would be a fabrication). If B carried half the
traffic and its slow tail is fat, the merged p95 can sit near 500ms; if B
carried 5 percent of traffic, the merged p95 is A's. *Averaging percentiles
across agents, across time windows, or across endpoints produces a
number with no definition.*

The fix is structural: keep the distribution, not the summary. Tools do this
with mergeable histogram sketches — HdrHistogram is the classic — which
record every sample into fixed-precision buckets that can be added together
exactly. loadr's trend metrics (`http_req_duration` and friends) are HDR
histograms end to end, which is what makes a distributed run's percentiles
identical to what a single agent would have measured; Gatling records into
HDR as well. If your tool's distributed mode reports per-agent percentiles
and leaves the merge to you, export raw samples or histograms and merge
those — never the percentiles.

=== Coordinated omission, demonstrated and corrected

Now the famous bug — the one Gil Tene spent years evangelizing about, the one
built into the default mode of most load tools ever written, and the one that
produces the most confidently wrong numbers in this field.

Picture a system that answers in 100ms, except that once during your
100-second test it stalls completely for 10 seconds — a GC pause, a failover,
a lock convoy. Two ways to measure it:

*The open world (what users do).* Users don't coordinate with your outage.
At 100 requests per second, about 1,000 requests arrive *during* the stall
and wait between 0 and 10 seconds extra. That's 10 percent of all traffic —
the honest p95 of this run is around *5 seconds*.

*The closed world (what most tests do).* A closed-model test — fixed virtual
users in a loop, each sending its next request only after the previous one
returns — coordinates perfectly with the outage. Take 10 VUs looping at
100ms per iteration: the same nominal 100 rps. When the stall hits, each VU
has exactly one request in flight. Those 10 requests take 10 seconds each,
get recorded, and then the loop resumes. Total damage in the data: 10 slow
samples out of roughly 9,000 — about 0.1 percent. The measured p99 of this
run is *100ms*. The tool didn't lie about any individual sample; it simply
failed to send the ~990 requests a real user population would have sent into
the stall, and so it never measured their suffering. The load generator
politely stopped testing at the exact moment the system started failing.

That's coordinated omission: *the samples you'd most want are the ones a
closed loop systematically declines to collect.* The only trace it leaves is
a throughput dip — which is why, in closed-model results, latency problems
masquerade as throughput problems.

The correction is to stop coordinating: drive load on a schedule the system
cannot influence. In loadr that's an arrival-rate executor:

[source,yaml]
----
# open model: 100 iterations/s start on the clock, ready or not
name: steady-open
defaults:
  http:
    base_url: https://api.example.com

scenarios:
  steady_api_load:
    executor: constant-arrival-rate
    rate: 100              # iterations per time_unit
    time_unit: 1s
    duration: 10m
    pre_allocated_vus: 50
    max_vus: 200

    flow:
      - request:
          name: health
          url: /health
          checks:
            - { type: status, equals: 200 }

thresholds:
  http_req_duration: [ "p(99)<300" ]
  dropped_iterations: [ "count<10" ]   # the honesty check — see below
----

The same idea in k6, because the concept is what matters:

[source,javascript]
----
export const options = {
  scenarios: {
    steady: {
      executor: 'constant-arrival-rate',   // open model
      rate: 100, timeUnit: '1s',
      duration: '10m',
      preAllocatedVUs: 50, maxVUs: 200,
    },
  },
  thresholds: {
    http_req_duration: ['p(99)<300'],
    dropped_iterations: ['count<10'],
  },
};
----

Note the second threshold in both. An open-model generator that runs out of
free VUs faces a choice: silently postpone the iteration (re-introducing the
coordination you were escaping) or *count the miss*. Both tools count it, as
`dropped_iterations`. That counter is your test's integrity check: if it's
climbing, the load you offered was less than the load you configured, and
every latency number in the run is now suspect. Gate on it. A stall that a
closed test would hide as quietly slower iterations shows up in an open test
as a spike in latency *and* a burst of dropped iterations — loud, twice.

[NOTE]
====
Closed models aren't wrong; they're wrong *for this*. When the real workload
is itself closed — a fixed worker pool draining a queue, a batch job with N
threads — a closed test is the faithful model, as <<ch03>> discusses. The sin
isn't running `constant-vus`. The sin is running it against an open system
(public web traffic) and reading the percentiles as if users wait politely
for each other.
====

If you're stuck with a closed-loop tool, there is a second-best correction:
HdrHistogram-style back-filling, which measures each sample from its
*intended* start time and synthesizes the missing samples during stalls
(this is what `wrk2` does). It recovers the honest tail. But if you can
choose the model instead of patching the measurement, choose the model.

=== Four pictures: histogram, CDF, latency over time, heatmap

A summary table is where analysis ends, not where it starts. Distributions
have *shapes*, and the shape is usually the diagnosis. Four views, in the
order I draw them:

*The histogram* answers "how many behaviors does this system have?" A healthy
single code path is one hump with a right tail. Two humps — like our
41ms/4.1s checkout — means two code paths (cache hit/miss, index used/table
scanned, pool hit/pool wait), and no single-number summary is meaningful
until you can name both. Chasing "high p99" without a histogram is how teams
optimize the fast path that was already fine.

*The CDF* (cumulative distribution) is the percentile-reading machine: x is
latency, y is the fraction of requests at or under it. Every percentile is
one horizontal read. Its killer feature is overlaying two runs — baseline and
candidate as two curves. A real regression shows as a horizontal gap over a
region of the curve, and *where* the gap sits tells you who is affected: a
gap from p50 upward means everyone got slower; curves glued together until
p97 with a gap above means you have a new rare event, and the median-focused
"it feels fine" report from staging is also true.

*Latency over time* answers "when?" — the dimension aggregates erase. The
end-of-run p95 of a 30-minute run cannot distinguish "uniformly mediocre"
from "excellent for 29 minutes, down for one." Plot p50/p95/p99 against
elapsed time and you'll see warm-up (exclude it from your aggregates — JIT,
caches, and pools make the first minute unrepresentative), degradation trends
(a p95 that climbs steadily through a soak is a leak or unbounded growth,
straight to <<ch13>>), and recovery — after a spike, does latency return to
baseline, or has the system been permanently wounded?

*The heatmap* is latency-over-time done properly: a 2D histogram, time on x,
latency buckets on y, color for count. It's the only one of the four that
shows the full distribution *at every moment*, so it catches what the p95
line smooths over — a second mode flickering in and out, a slow stripe
appearing exactly every 60 seconds (cron), the whole population jumping
between bands as an autoscaler flaps.

Here is a loadr plan that deliberately produces a shape worth looking at —
warm-up, steady baseline, a sharp spike to almost 5x the concurrency, and a
recovery window to verify the system comes back:

[source,yaml]
----
name: spike-shape
description: ramp -> spike -> recover, charted over time

defaults:
  http:
    base_url: https://shop.example.com

scenarios:
  browse:
    executor: ramping-vus
    start_vus: 0
    stages:
      - { duration: 10s, target: 25 }    # warm up
      - { duration: 10s, target: 25 }    # steady baseline
      - { duration: 5s,  target: 120 }   # spike
      - { duration: 10s, target: 120 }   # sustained spike
      - { duration: 5s,  target: 25 }    # back off
      - { duration: 10s, target: 25 }    # verify recovery
    flow:
      - request:
          name: feed
          url: /feed
          checks:
            - { type: status, equals: 200 }
      - think_time: { type: uniform, min: 100ms, max: 400ms }

thresholds:
  http_req_failed: [ "rate<0.05" ]
  http_req_duration: [ "p(95)<1500" ]
----

[source,console]
----
$ loadr run --summary-export results.json spike-shape.yaml
$ loadr report results.json -o report.html
----

The exported summary carries a `timeline` array — one point per snapshot
interval — alongside the exact end-of-run aggregates, and the generated HTML
report charts throughput, p50/p95/p99, active VUs, and error rate against
elapsed time. Every serious tool has an equivalent: k6 streams samples to a
time-series backend, JMeter's listeners feed Grafana, Gatling renders
response-time distribution and percentile-over-time charts in its HTML
report. The tool matters less than the habit: *look at the run over time
before you quote any aggregate about it.* And wire the same charts to the
server-side view (<<ch11>>) so "p99 spiked at 14:32:10" can meet "GC pause at
14:32:10" on one screen.

=== Comparing two runs honestly

Back to the opening problem: two runs, two numbers, one question. Before you
can compare runs, you have to know what a comparison can bear — and that
means confronting three things: variance, noise, and drift.

*Variance* is intrinsic: your load has randomized think times and data,
schedulers schedule, networks jitter. Even a perfect environment produces a
range, not a value.

*Noise* is environmental: a neighbor VM burning the shared CPU, a staging
database doing its backup, a burstable instance out of CPU credits, DNS
having a moment. Noise is why the 15-percent disagreement in this chapter's
opening existed at all.

*Drift* is slower and sneakier: the baseline itself rots. Test data grows
(this month's run queries a table 40 percent bigger than last month's),
dependencies get patched, the cloud rotates you onto a different hardware
generation at reprovision. A baseline from three weeks ago may be measuring
a system that no longer exists.

The single most useful thing you can do about all three costs one test run:
*measure your noise floor with an A/A comparison.* Run the identical plan
twice against the identical build, and diff the results as if they were a
release. Whatever deltas you see — say p95 wobbles ±6 percent — that is the
loudest silence your environment can produce, and no smaller delta between
A and B ever deserves a meeting. Teams that skip this step end up with a
regression gate tuned by superstition; teams that do it know exactly how big
a change their pipeline can detect, and either accept that resolution or
spend the effort (dedicated environment, longer holds, warm-up exclusion,
median of three runs) to buy a lower floor.

[WARNING]
====
Only compare runs of the *same plan and the same load shape*. A 10-VU run
versus a 100-VU run tells you nothing about the code change between them —
you varied two things and measured one. The same goes for comparing a
closed-model run against an open-model run, or a 2-minute smoke against a
30-minute soak. Baseline and candidate must differ in exactly one respect:
the system under test.
====

=== Detecting regressions automatically

Manual comparison doesn't scale past about one release, so automate it. The
shape of the automation is the same everywhere: export a machine-readable
summary of the baseline run, export one for the candidate, diff them with
direction-awareness (latency up is bad; throughput down is bad; error rate up
is bad), and fail loudly past a tolerance you chose on purpose.

loadr ships this as a first-class verb. Export both summaries, then diff:

[source,console]
----
$ loadr run perf/api.yaml --summary-export baseline.json    # e.g. on main
$ loadr run perf/api.yaml --summary-export current.json     # e.g. on the PR
$ loadr compare baseline.json current.json

  metric             field       baseline  current   delta     delta %
  -----------------  ----------  --------  --------  --------  --------  -
  http_req_duration  avg         52.10ms   55.80ms   +3.70ms   ▲ 7.1%
  http_req_duration  p50         41.00ms   42.30ms   +1.30ms   ▲ 3.2%
  http_req_duration  p95         98.40ms   131.20ms  +32.80ms  ▲ 33.3%   ✗
  http_req_duration  p99         180.00ms  184.10ms  +4.10ms   ▲ 2.3%    ✓
  http_reqs          per_second  1004.1/s  996.4/s   -7.8/s    ▼ 0.8%
  http_req_failed    error_rate  0.20%     0.21%     +0.01%    ▲ 5.0%    ✓
  checks             pass_rate   99.80%    99.75%    -0.05%    ▼ 0.1%    ✓
  thresholds         passed      pass      pass      -         -         ✓

✗ 1 regression(s) beyond tolerance
----

Read that table the way the tool does. Every row is direction-aware:
improvements are never flagged, and a threshold that passed in the baseline
but fails now is *always* a regression regardless of tolerance. By default
the gate watches the fields worth watching — p95, p99, error rate, check pass
rate — at a 5 percent relative tolerance, and everything else is
informational until you opt it in. And notice what the run itself would not
have told you: the plan's thresholds *passed* on both sides. "The thresholds
passed" and "this PR made p95 33 percent worse" are compatible statements,
which is exactly why a compare step exists.

Tune the tolerances to your measured noise floor, and make regressions fail
the pipeline:

[source,console]
----
$ loadr compare baseline.json current.json --assert \
    --max-regression p95=10% \
    --max-regression http_req_duration.p99=25 \
    --max-regression error_rate=0.5 \
    --max-regression rps=5% \
    --markdown compare.md
----

Percent limits are relative to the baseline; bare numbers are absolute in the
field's unit (`p99=25` allows +25ms, `error_rate=0.5` allows half a
percentage point). With `--assert`, any regression beyond tolerance exits
with code 99 — the same exit code as a threshold failure in `loadr run`, so
your existing CI wiring treats both identically — and `--markdown` produces a
paste-ready table for a PR comment. <<ch15>> builds the full pipeline around
this: where the baseline lives, how often it refreshes, and how to trend
results across weeks instead of only pairs of runs.

None of this is loadr-specific in concept. k6 gives you `handleSummary()` to
write the same kind of JSON export, and teams diff it with a short script or
feed a time-series backend and alert on deltas; JMeter and Gatling users
typically export to InfluxDB or Prometheus and gate in the dashboarding
layer. What matters is the contract: *every run leaves a machine-readable
artifact, and no human has to remember to look.*

[TIP]
====
Set your automated tolerance at roughly 1.5–2x your A/A noise floor. Tighter,
and the gate cries wolf until people stop reading it — the worst outcome,
because a muted alarm is worse than no alarm. Looser, and you're shipping the
regressions you built the gate to catch. If the resulting tolerance is too
loose to be useful (a 12 percent floor on a metric where you care about 5
percent), the fix is not a cleverer threshold; it's a quieter environment or
more samples.
====

=== Real regression or noisy environment?

The gate fired. Before anyone writes "perf regression" in a ticket, run the
checklist. It takes ten minutes and it has saved me weeks.

. *Is the delta bigger than your noise floor?* If A/A runs wobble ±6 percent
  and the flagged delta is 7 percent, you have weak evidence, not a verdict.
  Re-run before escalating. (If you don't know your noise floor, that — not
  the regression — is the first problem to fix.)
. *Is it consistent across the distribution?* A real code-path regression
  usually tells a coherent story: p50, p95, and p99 move together, or the
  tail moves alone because a new rare event appeared. One percentile jumping
  while its neighbors sleep is the signature of a handful of unlucky samples
  — check the sample count behind that percentile before believing it.
. *Is it consistent across endpoints?* Your change touched checkout. If
  checkout, catalog, *and* the health check all got 20 percent slower, the
  environment moved, not your code. Per-endpoint breakdowns (request `name`
  tags in loadr, k6, and Gatling alike) make this a thirty-second check.
. *Does it reproduce?* Run the candidate again. Real regressions are boringly
  repeatable; noise rarely strikes the same metric twice with the same
  magnitude. For gates you care about, compare medians of three runs rather
  than single runs — it costs machine time, which is cheap, and buys
  credibility, which isn't.
. *Does anything corroborate it server-side?* A real regression leaves
  fingerprints in the observability stack from <<ch11>> — CPU per request up,
  new query in the slow log, GC time up, a longer span in the trace. A
  client-side delta with a perfectly flat server is an invitation to suspect
  the load generator or the network path, not the application.

The Tuesday story, because every team has one. A nightly perf job "regressed"
every Tuesday for a month — p95 up 20 to 30 percent, always recovered by
Wednesday. Three engineers investigated three separate times; two PRs were
reverted, wrongly. The eventual answer was in nobody's diff: the shared
staging database's weekly full backup, scheduled by another team, in another
time zone, at exactly the hour the perf job ran. Every check on the list
above would have caught it — the delta cleared no measured noise floor
(nobody had measured one), every endpoint moved together, it never reproduced
on a Wednesday re-run, and the database host's I/O chart told the whole story
in one glance. The test results were never wrong. The reading was.

That's the chapter, and it's the book's thesis in miniature. Load
tests don't lie maliciously; they answer, precisely, a question slightly
different from the one you thought you asked. The average answers "what's the
mean of a distribution no user experiences?" The closed-model p99 answers
"how fast was the system when it was answering?" A single noisy run answers
"what happened once?" Reading results without fooling yourself is mostly the
craft of noticing which question your number actually answers — and asking a
better one.

.Key takeaways
****
* The average of a bimodal distribution describes a request that never
  happened. Report distributions — p50/p95/p99 and error rate at minimum —
  and treat a lone "average response time" as a red flag.
* Percentiles need samples: roughly 100 samples *beyond* the percentile
  before it's a measurement (p99 wants 10,000+). And they don't compose —
  never average percentiles across agents or windows; merge histograms
  (HDR-style) or raw samples instead.
* Coordinated omission means closed-loop tests systematically fail to sample
  the moments the system is worst — a 10-second stall can vanish from p99
  entirely. Prefer open (arrival-rate) load for open systems, and gate on
  `dropped_iterations` as the run's integrity check.
* Look at shape before summary: histograms reveal multiple code paths, CDF
  overlays localize a regression to the users it affects, latency-over-time
  reveals warm-up/leaks/recovery, heatmaps catch what percentile lines
  smooth over.
* Measure your noise floor with an A/A run before comparing anything, and
  only compare runs that differ in exactly one respect: the system under
  test.
* Automate the diff — direction-aware, tolerance-gated, machine-readable,
  failing the pipeline (`loadr compare --assert`, or the equivalent script in
  your stack). Then run the five-question checklist before believing any
  alarm: magnitude vs noise floor, consistency across percentiles,
  consistency across endpoints, reproducibility, server-side corroboration.
****

[.fieldcard]
.Field Card — Reading Results Honestly
****
Before you quote a number, know which question it actually answers — and whether your run had the samples to ask it.

Reporting latency:: never a lone mean; report p50/p95/p99 + error rate. The mean of a bimodal system describes a request no user made.
Trusting a percentile:: want ~100 samples *beyond* it — p95 wants ~2,000, p99 ~10,000, p99.9 ~100,000. Below that it's an anecdote.
Merging agents/windows:: never average percentiles — merge HDR histograms or raw samples. Averaging them makes a number with no definition.
Open system:: use `constant-arrival-rate` and gate `dropped_iterations`; a closed loop hides the stall as a throughput dip.
Comparing two runs:: same plan, same load shape, differing in exactly one thing — the build.

[source,console]
----
# measure the noise floor first: A/A, identical build
$ loadr run perf/api.yaml --summary-export baseline.json
$ loadr run perf/api.yaml --summary-export current.json
$ loadr compare baseline.json current.json --assert \
    --max-regression p95=10% --max-regression error_rate=0.5 \
    --markdown compare.md
----

*Remember:* set tolerance at 1.5–2x your A/A floor; `--assert` exits 99 past it. "Thresholds passed" and "p95 up 33%" are both true at once. Look at the shape (histogram, CDF, latency-over-time, heatmap) before quoting any aggregate.
****
