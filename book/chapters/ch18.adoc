== Building a Performance Culture

_Tools and tests fail without ownership. This is how they succeed._

The best performance-testing setup I ever saw belonged to a payments company,
and it died in fourteen months. They had everything this book teaches: a
workload model derived from production traffic, arrival-rate scenarios, SLO
thresholds gating every merge, a Grafana wall correlating load-test runs with
server-side metrics. The engineer who built it gave a conference talk about it.
Then she changed teams. Within a quarter the CI gate had been flipped to
warn-only "temporarily" during a crunch and never flipped back. Within two, the
dashboard's data source had rotted and the panels showed `N/A` — for four
months, and nobody filed a ticket, because nobody's job was to look at it.
Fourteen months after the conference talk, a checkout regression that the old
gate would have caught in a pull request instead took down Black Friday for
forty minutes.

Nothing in that story is a tooling failure. The scenarios were still correct.
The thresholds were still well-chosen. What failed was everything around the
tooling: ownership, visibility, and the boring institutional plumbing that
turns a good practice into a durable one. Chapters 1 through 16 taught you to
build tests whose numbers you can defend. This chapter is about making sure
someone is still running them — and still acting on them — two reorgs from now.

=== Who owns performance (why "everyone" means "no one")

Ask a room of engineers who owns performance and you'll get the answer that
sounds healthiest and works worst: "everyone." It's the diffusion of
responsibility in a hoodie. When everyone owns the p99, the p99 regresses and
everyone assumes someone else saw the graph. Social psychologists have a name
for this — the bystander effect — and it applies to dashboards exactly as it
applies to emergencies: the more people who _could_ act, the less likely any
individual does.

There are three ownership models you'll meet in the wild, and only two of them
work.

The *central performance team* — a dedicated group that runs all the load tests
— was the default in the 2000s and survives in some large enterprises. It
fails for the same reason centralized QA failed: the people running the tests
are furthest from the code, the tests happen late (usually as a pre-release
gate someone else has to schedule), and the service teams learn to treat
performance as somebody else's checkbox. When the central team says "your p95
regressed 40 percent," the response is a negotiation, not a fix.

The *embedded model* — each service team owns the performance of its service,
full stop — is where you want to end up. The team that wrote the code owns its
SLOs, runs its load tests in its own pipeline, gets paged when its latency
budget burns, and decides when performance work outranks feature work. This is
the same logic as "you build it, you run it," and it works for the same reason:
the feedback loop between causing a problem and feeling it is one team long.

The catch is that embedded ownership without support collapses into
twenty teams each half-learning JMeter. So the model that actually scales is
embedded ownership plus a small *enabling team*: two to four engineers who own
the shared tooling, the scenario conventions, the CI integration, and the
baseline infrastructure, and who parachute in for the genuinely hard problems —
the coordinated-omission bug, the 100,000-RPS distributed rig from <<ch10>>.
They are consultants and toolsmiths, not gatekeepers. The moment the enabling
team becomes the team that _runs_ the tests, you've rebuilt the central model
with extra steps.

Whichever shape you choose, one rule is non-negotiable: *every SLO has a named
owner.* Not a team alias — a person (the role can rotate; the name must exist).
The owner is who the burn-rate alert pages, who reviews changes to the
threshold file, and who stands up in the incident review when the budget is
gone. If you can't answer "who owns the checkout p99?" with a name in five
seconds, you've found your first culture bug, and it's cheaper to fix than any
regression it will eventually cause.

=== Performance budgets: a contract you can read in the repo

A performance budget is a number agreed *before* the work, enforced by a
machine, and visible to everyone it binds. All three clauses matter. A number
agreed after the work is a rationalization. A number enforced by a human is a
negotiation. A number nobody can see is folklore.

The term comes from frontend engineering — "this page ships no more than 300KB
of JavaScript" — but the idea generalizes to everything this book measures:
"checkout completes in 300ms at p99," "the search service holds 500 RPS inside
its SLO," "no release regresses p95 by more than 10 percent." You already know
how to express these; they're the thresholds from <<ch04>> and the regression
gates from <<ch15>>. The cultural move is to stop treating them as test
configuration and start treating them as *the contract* — versioned in the
repo, next to the code they constrain, changed only by pull request. In loadr,
that contract is the `thresholds` block of the plan CI already runs:

[source,yaml]
----
# perf/checkout.yaml (excerpt) — the checkout service's performance contract.
# Changing these numbers requires review from the SLO owner (see CODEOWNERS).
thresholds:
  # Service SLO: 99% of requests within 300ms, 99.9% within 1s.
  http_req_duration:
    - "slo(99%) < 300ms"
    - "slo(99.9%) < 1s"
  # Checkout is the money path; it gets a stricter per-endpoint budget.
  "http_req_duration{name:checkout}":
    - "slo(95%) < 250ms"
  # 99.9% availability, as an error-rate bound.
  http_req_failed: [ "rate<0.001" ]
----

Two properties make this block cultural infrastructure rather than mere config.
First, it's *diffable*: when someone loosens the checkout budget from 250ms to
400ms, that's a visible line in a pull request with a reviewer's name on the
approval — not a silent edit to a Jenkins job. Add `perf/` to
`CODEOWNERS` so the SLO owner is a required reviewer, and you have a complete
governance process in about six lines of configuration. Second, it's
*tool-neutral in spirit*: the same contract is a `thresholds` block in k6,

[source,javascript]
----
export const options = {
  thresholds: {
    http_req_duration: ['p(99)<300', 'p(99.9)<1000'],
    'http_req_duration{name:checkout}': ['p(95)<250'],
    http_req_failed: ['rate<0.001'],
  },
};
----

an `assertions` block in Gatling, or a `Duration` assertion in JMeter. The
syntax is fungible; the discipline — number first, machine-checked, in the repo
— is the point.

The second half of the contract is the *regression* budget: not "is it fast
enough in absolute terms" but "did this change make it worse." That's the
baseline-versus-candidate comparison from <<ch15>>, and it belongs in the same
pipeline that runs the unit tests:

[source,console]
----
$ loadr run perf/checkout.yaml --summary-export current.json
$ loadr compare baseline.json current.json --assert \
    --max-regression p95=10% \
    --max-regression error_rate=0.5 \
    --markdown compare.md          # posted as the PR comment
----

The `--markdown` output matters more than it looks. A regression gate that
fails with a wall of JSON gets `--no-verify`'d under deadline pressure. A gate
that fails with a readable table in the pull request — this endpoint, this
percentile, this much worse than baseline — gets fixed, because the cost of
understanding the failure just dropped below the cost of ignoring it. Culture
is downstream of ergonomics.

[TIP]
====
Budget the *headroom*, not just the limit. If the SLO is 300ms and the service
runs at 290ms, every threshold is green and you're one dependency bump from
red. Teams that track distance-to-budget on their dashboard treat 250→280ms as
the early warning it is, instead of discovering the problem at 301.
====

=== Dashboards and reporting that non-engineers trust

Engineers lose non-engineers with dashboards in three reliable ways: too many
numbers, unstable definitions, and no connection to anything the audience
values. The fix is not prettier charts. It's editorial discipline.

A performance dashboard for a mixed audience needs at most five numbers, and
each must survive three tests. *Stability:* the definition cannot change
month to month — the fastest way to teach a VP to distrust your dashboard is to
explain that p95 was measured differently last quarter, which is one more
reason the measurement rigor of <<ch12>> is load-bearing. *Legibility:* "99
percent of checkouts complete within 300ms" reads; `http_req_duration p(99)`
does not. Notice that the `slo(99%) < 300ms` spelling in the budget above
is deliberately the sentence, not the jargon — write the contract in the
language you'll report in. *Consequence:* every number should connect to money,
users, or risk. "Checkout latency budget: 62 percent consumed with 11 days left
in the window" is a sentence a CFO can act on. A latency histogram, however
beautiful, is not.

The layered pattern that works:

* *Engineers* get everything: the per-run HTML reports (`loadr report
  results.json -o report.html` renders the full timeline — throughput, p50/p95/p99,
  error rate over the run), the Grafana correlation from <<ch11>>, the PR
  comparison tables. Raw, dense, immediate.
* *The team* gets a single always-on view per service: SLO compliance, error
  budget remaining, and trend against budget for the two or three user journeys
  that matter. This is the screen the SLO owner is paid to look at.
* *Leadership* gets one page a month: each critical journey, green or red
  against its budget, the trend arrow, and one sentence per red item saying
  what's being done. No charts that need explaining. If it takes more than
  ninety seconds to read, it will not be read.

And one uncomfortable practice: *measure whether the dashboards are viewed.*
Grafana and most BI tools expose usage stats. A dashboard with zero views in
thirty days isn't reporting, it's decoration — archive it or fix the reason
nobody looks. The payments company from the opening had a wall of panels
showing `N/A` for four months. The panels weren't the failure; the four months
were.

=== Runbooks and on-call for performance regressions

Performance regressions make terrible pages, and that's exactly why they need
on-call discipline. An outage announces itself; a regression seeps. Latency
drifts up 8 percent a week for a month, each week's delta lost in daily noise,
until — as <<ch01>> put it — slow is the on-ramp to down. The teams that
catch the drift are the ones that alert on *error-budget burn rate* rather than
instantaneous latency: "at the current rate, this journey exhausts its 30-day
budget in four days" fires early, resists flapping, and arrives with its own
priority attached. Point-in-time threshold alerts ("p99 > 300ms for 5
minutes"), by contrast, either page you nightly for noise or stay silent
through a slow bleed. <<ch16>> covers the mechanics; the cultural half is what
happens after the page — and that's the runbook.

A performance runbook is not a diagnosis manual (that's <<ch13>>). It's the
document that gets a responder from "the burn alert fired" to "I know what
changed and whether to roll back" at 3 a.m. without waking the one engineer who
understands the service. The one-page version:

* *What this SLI is*, in one sentence, and where it's measured (client, edge,
  server — they differ, and responders waste hours discovering that).
* *Links that work:* the SLO dashboard, the load-test history, the last known
  baseline. Test the links quarterly; a runbook full of 404s is worse than none
  because it burns trust at the worst moment.
* *What changed:* the query or command listing deploys, feature-flag flips, and
  config changes for this service and its hot dependencies in the last 48
  hours. The majority of regressions are answered right here.
* *First moves:* the three or four discriminating checks from <<ch13>> —
  saturation signals, downstream latency, cache hit rate — in order.
* *Rollback criteria, pre-agreed:* "if checkout p99 exceeds 500ms for 15
  minutes and a deploy is in the change window, roll back first and diagnose
  after." Deciding this in a document beats deciding it in an argument.
* *Who to call*, by name and escalation order.

The deeper cultural point: *treat a latency regression as a defect with a
severity, not as an aspiration with a backlog ticket.* A team that would never
ship a wrong answer will happily ship a slow one and file "investigate checkout
latency" into a backlog where it composts. The budget file makes this
enforceable — a burned budget is a failed contract, and failed contracts get
incident numbers, owners, and reviews, exactly like functional outages.

=== SLO governance without bureaucracy

Say "governance" and engineers picture a monthly SLO Steering Committee with a
40-page charter, which is why so many teams have no governance at all. Both
failure modes kill the practice — one by suffocation, one by neglect. The
minimum viable version is three rules, none of which requires a meeting:

1. *Every SLO has a named owner.* (Covered above; it's the keystone.)
2. *Changing an SLO is a pull request.* The budget lives in the repo; the owner
   is a required reviewer; the diff and the rationale are permanent record.
   Loosening a budget is sometimes the right call — a mis-set target from
   <<ch04>>, a genuine product trade-off — but it should cost one honest
   paragraph of justification. What it should never be is silent.
3. *Every SLO gets reviewed on a calendar, quarterly.* Thirty minutes, three
   questions per SLO: Did it page us usefully or noisily? Does it still track
   what users feel? Did we burn budget, and did the agreed policy actually
   trigger? An SLO that has paged nobody and blocked nothing in two quarters is
   either mis-set or measuring something nobody cares about — retire or retune
   it.

The other half of lightweight governance is the *error-budget policy*: the
pre-agreed answer to "the budget is gone, now what?" The classic version —
budget exhausted means feature work pauses for reliability work until the
budget recovers — is less important than the pre-agreement itself. Negotiating
consequences during the incident guarantees the loudest voice wins; writing
them down in peacetime, with engineering and product both signing, means the
prioritization argument happens once instead of every time.

[WARNING]
====
Resist SLO sprawl. The team that defines forty SLOs in an enthusiastic
afternoon reviews none of them by the following quarter. Start with the three
to five user journeys where slowness costs real money or real trust —
checkout, search, login — and earn your way to more. An SLO you don't review,
alert on, and defend is a lie with a dashboard.
====

=== Getting performance work into the plan

Here is where performance culture actually lives or dies — not in the tooling,
but in sprint planning, because performance work has a structural disadvantage
there: its benefits are diffuse and preventative while feature work is concrete
and demandable. Left to gladiatorial prioritization, "tune the connection pool"
loses to "ship the thing sales promised" every single sprint, right up until
the outage. You don't fix that with advocacy. You fix it with mechanisms that
remove the recurring argument:

*Put the budget check in the definition of done.* A feature isn't finished
when the functional tests pass; it's finished when the budget file still
passes. Because the gate runs in CI on every pull request (<<ch15>>), this
costs nothing at planning time — the regression is discovered while the change
is one diff, one author, one afternoon old, which is two orders of magnitude
cheaper than discovering it in production a quarter later.

*Ask the performance question in refinement.* One question, ten seconds, added
to whatever refinement checklist you already use: _does this change touch a hot
path, add a query, call a new dependency, or change payload sizes?_ If yes, the
story gets a performance acceptance criterion and, for the genuinely risky
ones, a scenario update. Most stories answer no and cost you nothing. The
occasional yes is precisely the change that was going to be your next incident.

*Let the error budget do the prioritizing.* This is the elegance of the SLO
frame: when the budget is healthy, product wins the arguments and that's
correct — the system is demonstrably reliable enough, and gold-plating it has
real opportunity cost. When the budget burns, the pre-agreed policy moves
reliability work into the sprint without anyone having to win a fight. The
same number that gates releases arbitrates the roadmap.

*Schedule capacity reviews before you need them.* Big launches, seasonal
peaks, and marketing pushes are known in advance; the capacity math of
<<ch17>> takes days when scheduled and weeks when panicked. A one-hour review
six weeks out — current headroom, projected load, sweep results, scaling plan —
is the cheapest insurance in this book.

=== Anti-patterns: three ways to have the artifacts and lose the plot

You can adopt every practice above as theater. Here are the three most common
ways, so you can recognize them from inside.

*Pre-release load-test theater.* The organization runs one big load test per
year, the week before Black Friday. It fails in every dimension at once: the
scenario is unrepresentative because nobody's maintained the workload model
since last year (<<ch03>>); there's no baseline, so nobody can say whether the
numbers are good or merely numbers (<<ch04>>); and the results land five days
before an immovable date, so the only findings that get fixed are the ones
fixable in five days — the rest get a risk-acceptance email. The test isn't
functioning as a test. It's functioning as a ritual that lets everyone say
performance was checked. The tell: ask what the team would do differently if
the test _failed_. If the honest answer is "ship anyway," you're watching
theater. The fix is the entire thesis of <<ch15>>: many small continuous tests
beat one heroic annual one, and the annual test's real job — full-scale
validation — only works when it's confirming what the pipeline already knows.

*The ignored dashboard.* Every panel green or `N/A`, zero views in thirty
days, and a team that sincerely believes it "has monitoring." A dashboard is
not a practice; looking at it is. If nobody's calendar, on-call rotation, or
review ritual includes the dashboard, it's a screensaver with a data source —
and worse than nothing, because it lets everyone believe the watching is
happening. Assign it: the SLO owner opens the budget view weekly, the quarterly
review walks through it, and any panel that's been `N/A` for a week files its
own ticket.

*The vanity benchmark.* The number that exists to be impressive rather than
informative: hello-world at a million RPS on hardware production will never
see, the synthetic endpoint that skips auth and the database, the
tool-versus-tool bake-off that measures the load generators instead of the
system. Vanity benchmarks are worse than useless because they *calibrate
intuition wrong* — the team "knows" the platform handles 1M RPS, so the
capacity review gets skipped, and the real system falls over at twelve thousand
because real requests hold real locks. The test from <<ch06>>: a legitimate
benchmark measures a defined question about a production-representative path,
and its author can tell you what decision changes if the number comes back
worse. If nobody can name that decision, it's marketing.

The thread through all three: *artifacts without owners decay into theater.* A
test nobody would fail the release over, a dashboard nobody reads, a benchmark
nobody would act on — each has the form of the practice and none of the
function. Which is the payments company's story, and the reason this chapter
exists.

=== The flywheel

Culture sounds like the soft stuff, but look back at what this chapter actually
prescribed: a name in a CODEOWNERS file, a YAML contract in the repo, a gate in
the pipeline, a markdown table in the pull request, a page-per-month report, a
runbook with working links, a thirty-minute quarterly review. Culture, it turns
out, is mostly *artifacts plus habits* — small, checkable, boring things that
survive the departure of whoever built them. That's the test the payments
company failed and the test your setup should pass: if your best performance
engineer leaves tomorrow, does the p99 still have a guardian? If the answer is
a named owner, a versioned budget, and a pipeline that says no — then yes, it
does.

Everything else in this book was about running tests whose numbers you can
defend. This chapter was about building a place where you'll still be running
them next year. Do both, and performance stops being a heroic quarterly effort
and becomes what it should have been all along: a property of the system,
measured on every change, owned by someone whose name you know.

.Key takeaways
****
* "Everyone owns performance" means no one does. Embed ownership in service
  teams, support them with a small enabling team, and give every SLO a *named*
  owner — a person, not an alias.
* A performance budget is a number agreed before the work, enforced by a
  machine, and versioned in the repo. Changing it is a pull request with the
  owner as reviewer — that's your entire governance process.
* Report in layers: everything for engineers, budget-and-trend for the team,
  one legible page for leadership. Stable definitions and a connection to
  money or users are what make non-engineers trust the numbers.
* Alert on error-budget burn rate, not point-in-time latency, and treat a
  regression as a defect with a severity — backed by a runbook that gets a
  3 a.m. responder from alert to rollback decision without a hero.
* Mechanisms beat advocacy in planning: budget checks in the definition of
  done, one performance question in refinement, and an error-budget policy
  that moves reliability work into the sprint automatically.
* Artifacts without owners decay into theater. The annual pre-release test,
  the unread dashboard, and the vanity benchmark all have the form of the
  practice and none of the function.
****
