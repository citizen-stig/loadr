[[ch02]]
== The Language of Performance

A war room, 2 a.m. The checkout service is timing out and someone has just put
the API dashboard on the screen. "The API is fine — latency is 45 milliseconds."
Across the table: "Then why are checkouts taking eleven seconds?" Both
statements were true. The first person meant the *average service time*
measured *inside* the server, after the request had cleared a queue that was,
at that moment, four thousand requests deep. The second meant the *response
time* experienced by a browser on the other side of that queue, at the *99th
percentile*. They weren't disagreeing about the system. They were speaking two
different languages and didn't know it.

This chapter is the phrasebook. It's short on tooling and long on definitions,
because every dispute I've ever refereed about a load-test result — every
"your test must be broken," every "but staging was fine" — eventually reduced
to two people using the same word for different quantities. Get these
definitions straight and the later chapters get dramatically easier. Get them
wrong and every number the tools print for you will be technically accurate
and practically a lie.

There are also five load-bearing ideas here — percentiles, Little's Law,
coordinated omission, saturation, and the SLI/SLO frame — that the rest of the
book leans on constantly. <<ch12>> makes them rigorous. This chapter makes
them usable.

=== Latency, response time, and service time

Queueing theory settled this vocabulary decades ago, and it's worth adopting
its precision even though the industry won't.

*Service time* is how long the system spends actually working on your request
— parsing it, hitting the database, rendering the response. It's a property of
the code and the data, and it's what a profiler measures.

*Wait time* (or queue time) is how long the request sits around before and
between bits of service — in an accept queue, a thread pool's backlog, a
connection pool's waiting list, a load balancer's buffer.

*Response time* is what the client experiences: wait time plus service time,
plus the network in both directions. This is the number your users feel and
the number a load test measures from the outside.

"Latency," in the wild, means any of these depending on who's talking. Server
dashboards usually report something close to service time; browser and
load-test tooling report response time. That gap is precisely the queue, and
under load the queue is where all the interesting behavior lives — which is
why the 2 a.m. argument above happens so often. A server can honestly report
45ms of service time while clients honestly experience 11 seconds, and the
missing 10.955 seconds is requests waiting their turn.

[NOTE]
====
In this book, "latency" and "response time" both mean the client-observed,
end-to-end number unless explicitly qualified. When I mean the server-side
component I'll say *service time*. Adopt the same discipline in your incident
channels; it's free and it prevents the worst arguments.
====

Load-testing tools decompose response time further, and the decomposition is
diagnostic gold. loadr, like k6, records the phases of every HTTP request as
separate metrics:

[cols="2,3",options="header"]
|===
| Metric | What it measures
| `http_req_blocked` | waiting for a connection — DNS plus TCP/TLS on cold connections, ~0 on reuse
| `http_req_connecting` | TCP handshake
| `http_req_tls_handshaking` | TLS negotiation
| `http_req_sending` | writing the request
| `http_req_waiting` | first byte wait — the server thinking (TTFB)
| `http_req_receiving` | reading the response body
| `http_req_duration` | sending + waiting + receiving
|===

Note what `http_req_duration` deliberately excludes: connection setup. If your
`http_req_blocked` numbers climb during a test, the bottleneck may be the load
generator's own connection pool, not your server — a self-inflicted result
we'll return to in <<ch10>>. And a fat `http_req_receiving` with a slim
`http_req_waiting` means the server was fast and the payload was huge, which
points at a very different fix than a slow query.

=== Throughput, and its honest cousin goodput

*Throughput* is work completed per unit time — for HTTP, usually requests per
second (RPS). It answers "how much," where latency answers "how long," and the
central drama of this entire field is that the two are coupled: past a certain
point, pushing throughput up drags latency up with it, gently at first and
then catastrophically. We'll draw that curve shortly.

Two refinements keep throughput numbers honest. First, distinguish *requests*
per second from *iterations* per second. A realistic scenario — log in, browse,
add to cart, check out — is one iteration containing many requests. loadr
reports both (`http_reqs` and `iterations`); when someone quotes "the system
does 5,000 per second," make them say 5,000 *what*.

Second, count only work that succeeded. A server returning `503` in two
milliseconds can post spectacular RPS while serving no one. Throughput minus
errors is sometimes called *goodput*, and goodput is the number you actually
care about. A stress test that drives a system past its limit will routinely
show total throughput rising while goodput collapses — the system is "doing"
more and accomplishing less.

=== Two ways to say "how much load": virtual users and arrival rate

Here is the first concept in this book that separates people who run load
tests from people who understand them.

A *closed* workload model fixes the number of concurrent workers — *virtual
users*, VUs — and each one loops: send a request, wait for the response, maybe
think for a bit, repeat. The offered load is a *consequence* of how fast the
system responds. If the system slows down, each VU's loop takes longer, so the
VUs send *fewer* requests. The load politely backs off exactly when the system
struggles.

An *open* workload model fixes the *arrival rate* — new requests per second —
regardless of how the system is coping. This is how the real internet behaves:
the users arriving at your site this second did not consult the users already
waiting. If the system slows down, arrivals keep coming, the queue grows, and
the pain compounds. The load does *not* back off.

Both models are legitimate; they answer different questions (<<ch03>> treats
the choice in depth). What is not legitimate is running a closed test and
quoting the result as if it were open — which is, unfortunately, the default
mistake, because VUs are the default abstraction in most tools.

In loadr the two models are different executors. Closed:

[source,yaml]
----
scenarios:
  browse:
    executor: ramping-vus          # closed model: N workers looping
    start_vus: 0
    stages:
      - { duration: 2m, target: 50 }   # ramp up
      - { duration: 5m, target: 50 }   # hold
      - { duration: 1m, target: 0 }    # ramp down
    think_time: { type: uniform, min: 1s, max: 4s }
    flow:
      - request:
          name: products
          url: /products
          checks: [ { type: status, equals: 200 } ]
----

Open:

[source,yaml]
----
scenarios:
  steady_api_load:
    executor: constant-arrival-rate  # open model: 100 starts/s, no matter what
    rate: 100
    time_unit: 1s
    duration: 10m
    pre_allocated_vus: 50            # workers available to absorb slowness
    max_vus: 200                     # hard ceiling
    flow:
      - request:
          name: health
          url: /health
          checks: [ { type: status, equals: 200 } ]

thresholds:
  dropped_iterations: [ "count<10" ]   # the canary — see below
----

The open executor still needs workers under the hood, and that detail matters:
if the system slows so much that all `max_vus` workers are busy when the next
scheduled arrival comes due, that arrival can't start. loadr counts these as
`dropped_iterations`, and the threshold above turns any significant number of
them into a test failure. A nonzero
`dropped_iterations` means one of two things: your `max_vus` is too small, or
the system under test is drowning — and either way, the latency numbers from
that run understate reality.

For contrast, here's the closed model in its most natural habitat — Locust,
where a "user" is literally a Python class that loops and thinks:

[source,python]
----
from locust import HttpUser, task, between

class ShopUser(HttpUser):
    wait_time = between(1, 4)   # think time: the closed-model heartbeat

    @task
    def browse(self):
        self.client.get("/products")
----

Same idea, different spelling. Every serious tool can express both models —
k6 has `ramping-vus` and `ramping-arrival-rate` executors, Gatling has
`atOnceUsers`/`rampUsers` versus `constantUsersPerSec`. The vocabulary you need
is the model, not the flag.

=== Little's Law, the pocket calculator

One equation from queueing theory earns a permanent place in your head:

[source,text]
----
L = λ × W

concurrency = throughput × response time
----

In steady state, the average number of requests *in the system* (L) equals the
arrival rate (λ) times the average time each request spends in the system (W).
No assumptions about distributions, arrival patterns, or architecture. It is
always true, and that makes it the best lie detector in performance testing.

Use it three ways.

*Sanity-check any result.* A report claims 500 requests per second at an
average response time of 200ms. Little's Law says that run averaged
500 × 0.2 = 100 requests in flight. If the test was configured with 10 VUs in
a closed loop, that's impossible — a closed loop of 10 workers can never have
more than 10 requests in flight. Something in the report is wrong: the rate,
the latency, or your understanding of the test. I have caught mislabeled
dashboards, unit errors (ms versus s), and outright broken aggregation with
thirty seconds of this arithmetic, in meetings, repeatedly.

*Size an open-model test.* Want 100 iterations per second against a flow whose
iterations take ~300ms on a healthy system? You'll have 100 × 0.3 = 30 workers
busy on average — so `pre_allocated_vus: 50` gives comfortable headroom and
`max_vus: 200` leaves room for the system to degrade 6x before the test itself
becomes the constraint. That is exactly how the numbers in the example above
were chosen.

*Reason about capacity.* A service holds 200 concurrent requests before its
thread pool exhausts, and its SLO caps response time at 400ms. Little's Law
puts its maximum SLO-compliant throughput at 200 / 0.4 = 500 rps. No load test
required for the first-order estimate — and when the load test disagrees with
it, one of your assumptions just became visible. <<ch16>> builds capacity
planning on this foundation.

[TIP]
====
Little's Law works at every scope: a connection pool, a service, a whole
platform. Whenever you meet a triple of concurrency, throughput, and latency,
multiply two and check the third. It costs nothing and it catches everything
from typos to fabricated results.
====

=== Percentiles, done properly

<<ch01>> made the argument that averages hide the users who matter. The
positive statement of that argument: report *percentiles*. The p95 is the
value below which 95 percent of samples fall; if p95 is 480ms, then one
request in twenty took longer. p50 (the median) describes the typical
experience; p95 and p99 describe the experience of your busiest, most
fan-out-exposed, most valuable users; p99.9 describes the experience that
generates support tickets.

Percentiles come with one rule that is violated on more dashboards than it is
respected:

*You cannot average percentiles.* The average of per-minute p95s is not the
p95 of the hour. The average of ten servers' p99s is not the fleet's p99. A
percentile is a point on a distribution, and distributions don't combine by
averaging points — a node handling 10 requests and a node handling 10,000
don't get equal votes. The only correct way to combine percentile data is to
merge the underlying distributions and *recompute*.

[WARNING]
====
This is the most common way honest data becomes a dishonest dashboard. If a
graph is built by taking `avg()` over pre-computed p95 values — per minute, per
node, per shard — its line can sit comfortably under your SLO while the true
fleet-wide p95 sits far above it. When you inherit a dashboard, find out what
the aggregation function is *before* you trust a single panel.
====

Merging distributions is what histograms are for. A histogram sorts samples
into buckets and counts them, which compresses millions of samples into
kilobytes while preserving the shape of the distribution — and two histograms
with the same buckets merge losslessly, by adding counts. The refinement used
by serious tools is the *HDR (High Dynamic Range) histogram*, which sizes its
buckets logarithmically so that it holds everything from microseconds to
minutes in one compact structure with a guaranteed relative precision. loadr
records every trend metric into HDR histograms at three significant figures;
in distributed runs, each agent streams delta histograms to the controller,
which merges them centrally — so the p99 you see from a twenty-node run is the
true p99 of all twenty nodes' samples, not an average of averages. Whatever
tool you use, this is a property to demand: ask *where the percentile is
computed* and *what got merged to produce it*. If the answer involves
averaging percentiles at any stage, the number is decorative.

=== Coordinated omission: the lie your own tool tells

Now for the trap that earned a name, because it silently corrupts closed-model
results in exactly the runs that matter most. Gil Tene called it *coordinated
omission*: the load generator and the system under test cooperating —
accidentally — to omit the worst samples from the record.

Recall how a closed-model VU works: send, *wait for the response*, repeat.
Now walk through a concrete run.

You test a service for 100 seconds with one VU, no think time between
one-per-second paced requests. Healthy response time: 1ms. But at t=50s the
service stalls completely for 50 seconds — a GC pause, a failover, a lock
convoy. What does the VU record?

* Fifty samples of ~1ms (t=0 through t=49).
* At t=50 it sends a request and waits... and waits. One sample of 50,000ms.
* The test ends.

Fifty-one samples: fifty at 1ms, one at 50s. The summary reports an average of
~980ms, a p50 of 1ms, and — here is the punchline — a *p90 of 1ms*. Nine out
of ten requests were instant! The max looks like a lone outlier, and lone
outliers get waved away.

Now consider what real users would have experienced. In an open world, users
keep arriving during the stall — one per second, per our intended pacing. The
user arriving at t=51 waits 49 seconds. The user at t=52 waits 48. And so on:
fifty users experiencing 50s, 49s, ... down to 1s. The honest dataset is 100
samples — fifty at 1ms and fifty smeared from 1s to 50s. The true p50 is about
a second. The true p75 is about 25 seconds. The true p90 is about *40
seconds*.

Measured p90: 1ms. Actual p90: 40 seconds. The measurement isn't slightly off;
it is wrong by four orders of magnitude, and it is wrong in the flattering
direction, and it is *most* wrong precisely when the system misbehaves — the
one time you needed the test to tell the truth. The VU couldn't send during
the stall, so the stall's victims simply never appear in the data. The
omission is coordinated with the outage.

Two defenses, in order of preference:

1. *Use an open model for measurement.* An arrival-rate executor keeps
   scheduling iterations on time; slow responses pile up as genuinely
   concurrent requests, and each one's full wait is recorded. When even that
   breaks down — no free workers left — loadr's `dropped_iterations` counter
   tells you your numbers have a blind spot, rather than silently narrowing
   the dataset. A failed measurement that announces itself is fine. A wrong
   one that doesn't is not.
2. *If you must use a closed model with pacing, correct for it.* Some tools
   can back-fill the samples that the schedule says should have been sent
   (wrk2 pioneered this; JMeter and Gatling have pacing/timer configurations
   that reduce, though don't eliminate, the effect). Correction is better than
   nothing and worse than not needing it.

<<ch12>> shows how to detect coordinated omission in results you didn't
generate yourself — there's a telltale signature in the shape of the
histogram. For now, adopt the reflex: *whenever you see a beautiful percentile
from a closed-model test, ask what the VUs were doing while the system was at
its worst.*

=== Saturation, utilization, and the knee of the curve

Plot response time against offered load for any real system and you get the
same shape every time: flat, flat, flat, slightly up, *cliff*.

The x-axis is best understood through *utilization* — the fraction of a
resource's capacity in use. Queueing theory's classic result is that average
wait time grows in proportion to ρ/(1−ρ), where ρ is utilization. The
denominator is the whole story. At 50 percent utilization the penalty factor
is 1; at 80 percent it's 4; at 90 percent, 9; at 95 percent, 19; at 99
percent, 99. Each step toward "full" costs more than the last, because a
busier server makes each arrival more likely to queue behind others. The
curve has a *knee* — the region, typically between 70 and 85 percent
utilization for systems with bursty arrivals, where latency stops being flat
and starts climbing steeply. Past the knee lives *saturation*: work is
arriving faster than it can be served, queues grow without bound, and
response time is governed by queue depth, not by your code.

Three practical consequences:

* *"We're at 60 percent CPU, we have plenty of headroom" is only half a
  sentence.* Headroom until the knee, not until 100 percent. A system
  provisioned to run at 90 percent utilization at peak is a system
  provisioned to fall over during any burst.
* *Capacity testing is knee-finding.* The question isn't "what's the maximum
  RPS" — the maximum sits in a regime you never want to visit. The question is
  where the knee is, because the knee is your real capacity.
* *Averages are at their most deceptive near the knee*, because the
  distribution goes bimodal — some requests skate through, others queue — and
  the average lands between the two humps, describing nobody.

Knee-finding in practice means stepping load and comparing distributions at
each step, which is tedious to do by hand and exactly the kind of tedium worth
automating. loadr's `sweep` runs one plan across a parameter matrix and
tabulates the percentiles side by side, one row per load level:

[source,console]
----
$ loadr sweep capacity.yaml --var vus=10,25,50,100,200 --duration 5m
----

In the resulting table, find the first row where p99 leaves the flat region:
the knee is between that row and the one before it. <<ch16>>
turns this into a full capacity-planning method.

=== What counts as an error?

"Error rate: 0.02%" sounds like the least ambiguous number on the summary. It
is one of the most ambiguous, because *error* is a definition you chose,
usually implicitly, usually by accepting a tool's default.

loadr's default, shared with k6: `http_req_failed` counts transport failures
(connection refused, reset, timeout) and any HTTP status of 400 or above. A
reasonable default — and insufficient in three directions.

*Errors that hide inside 200s.* An endpoint that returns `200 OK` with
`{"results": []}` because a downstream dependency died is failing its users
while polishing your error rate. The fix is *checks* — assertions on the
response body, not just the status:

[source,yaml]
----
- request:
    name: search
    url: /search?q=boots
    checks:
      - { type: status, equals: 200 }
      - { type: jsonpath, name: has results, expression: "$.results[0]", exists: true }
----

Failed checks are recorded on their own `checks` rate metric, so "the server
answered" and "the answer was right" stay separately visible. Keep them
separate in your head, too: they fail for different reasons and get fixed by
different teams.

*Successes that are too slow to count.* A response that arrives after the
client gave up is a failure, whatever its status code. If your callers time
out at 2 seconds, a 2.5-second `200` is an error, and your test should say so —
this is what latency SLOs (next section) are for.

*The 4xx question.* Whether client errors count depends on the test. During a
correctness-oriented run, a `404` is a bug in your scenario. During a
realistic-traffic run, a background rate of 4xx *is the workload* — real
traffic includes bad requests, and a system that's never tested with them has
an untested code path. Decide per test, write the decision down, and make sure
everyone reading the report knows which convention it used.

=== Two mental models: USE and RED

When a test surfaces a problem, you need a checklist for *where to look*, and
two complementary ones have become standard.

*USE* — Utilization, Saturation, Errors — is Brendan Gregg's method, applied
*per resource*. For every resource (CPU, memory, disk I/O, network, connection
pools, thread pools): how busy is it, how much work is queued for it, and is
it erroring? USE looks at the system from the inside, and it's the fastest
route to "which resource is the bottleneck."

*RED* — Rate, Errors, Duration — is Tom Wilkie's method, applied *per
service*: how many requests, how many failing, how long they take. RED looks
at the system from the outside, the way its consumers see it, and it maps
one-to-one onto what a load test measures — rate is your throughput, errors
your error rate, duration your latency distribution.

They pair naturally: RED tells you *which service* is hurting; USE tells you
*which resource inside it* is the cause. A load test is essentially a machine
for turning RED dials while you watch USE dashboards, and <<ch11>> wires the
two together properly.

=== SLIs, SLOs, SLAs, and the budget for being wrong

The last piece of vocabulary turns everything above into decisions.

An *SLI* — service level indicator — is a measurement you've chosen to care
about: "the fraction of checkout requests completing successfully in under
300ms." Good SLIs are ratios of good events to total events, measured as
close to the user as practical.

An *SLO* — service level objective — is a target for an SLI over a window:
"99.9 percent of checkout requests complete successfully in under 300ms,
measured over 30 days." This is the load-bearing artifact. It is where "fast
enough" stops being a feeling and becomes a number that a test can pass or
fail. <<ch04>> is devoted to writing good ones.

An *SLA* — service level agreement — is an SLO with consequences attached:
a contract, usually with refunds or penalties. Engineering teams work to
SLOs; lawyers work to SLAs; the SLO is typically stricter than the SLA so that
you feel the pain before the customer invokes the contract.

The *error budget* is the SLO's complement, reframed as an allowance: 99.9
percent over 30 days means you may fail 0.1 percent — about 43 minutes of
total badness a month. The reframing matters because it converts reliability
from a virtue ("more nines are better") into a resource you spend. Budget
left over? Ship faster, run riskier experiments. Budget exhausted? Feature
work pauses and reliability work takes over. It's the negotiating table where
velocity and reliability meet with numbers instead of adjectives.

You may also meet *Apdex*, an older single-number score: pick a threshold T,
count responses under T as satisfied, between T and 4T as tolerating (half
credit), beyond 4T as frustrated (none), and average. It compresses a
distribution into one number between 0 and 1 — which is both its appeal and
its flaw, since wildly different distributions can share an Apdex score.
Percentile SLOs have largely superseded it; you'll still find it in APM
products, and it's fine as a trend line so long as nobody gates a release
on it.

Because SLOs are how reliability targets are actually written, a load test
should be able to state its pass/fail gate in the same words. In loadr,
thresholds accept SLO phrasing directly — "N percent of requests within the
bound" — alongside the classic percentile syntax:

[source,yaml]
----
thresholds:
  # The service SLO, stated the way the reliability doc states it:
  http_req_duration:
    - "slo(99%) < 300ms"        # same gate as p(99) < 300
    - "slo(99.9%) < 1s"
  # A stricter SLO for the endpoint that makes the money:
  "http_req_duration{name:checkout}":
    - "slo(95%) < 250ms"
  # 99.9% availability, as an error-rate bound:
  http_req_failed: [ "rate<0.001" ]
----

The same gate in Gatling, as assertions on the simulation:

[source,scala]
----
setUp(scn.inject(constantUsersPerSec(100).during(10.minutes)))
  .assertions(
    global.responseTime.percentile(99).lt(300),
    global.failedRequests.percent.lt(0.1)
  )
----

Different spellings; one idea. The SLO is written down once, in the language
of the people who own the service, and every test, dashboard, and alert
checks *that*, so a passing test means the same thing as a healthy dashboard.
When the test's gate and the production SLO drift apart, the test starts
certifying something nobody promised — which is how systems pass every test
and still breach their contracts.

.Key takeaways
****
* *Response time = wait + service.* Server dashboards see service time;
  users experience response time; under load, the difference — the queue — is
  where the story is.
* Load comes in two models: *closed* (fixed VUs, load backs off when the
  system slows) and *open* (fixed arrival rate, load keeps coming). Know
  which one you're running, because they answer different questions.
* *Little's Law* (`concurrency = throughput × response time`) is a free lie
  detector: any report that violates it is wrong somewhere.
* Percentiles describe users; averages describe nobody. And *you cannot
  average percentiles* — merge histograms (HDR, ideally) and recompute.
* *Coordinated omission* makes closed-model tests flattest exactly when the
  system is at its worst. Prefer open models for measurement, and treat
  `dropped_iterations`-style counters as the test confessing its blind spots.
* Systems don't degrade linearly: latency follows ρ/(1−ρ) and turns at a
  *knee* around 70–85 percent utilization. Capacity means "where's the knee,"
  not "what's the max."
* "Error" is a definition you choose: transport failures, bad statuses,
  wrong answers inside good statuses, and too-slow successes are all
  different failures. Choose explicitly.
* *RED* (per service) plus *USE* (per resource) is the diagnostic pairing:
  RED finds the hurting service, USE finds the exhausted resource.
* SLIs are measurements, SLOs are targets, SLAs are contracts, and the error
  budget is the SLO turned into a spendable resource. Write your test gates
  in the same language as your SLOs, or your tests certify the wrong promise.
****
