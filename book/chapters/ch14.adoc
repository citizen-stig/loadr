== Performance Under Attack: Security and Resilience Testing

A load test and a denial-of-service attack send the same packets. Same connections, same request shapes, same pressure on the same CPU. The only difference is written on your face, not on the wire: one of you is trying to find the breaking point so you can move it, and the other is trying to find it so they can stand on it. The tooling doesn't know which one you are. That's the uncomfortable truth this chapter is built on, and it's why we spend the first paragraph on rules before we spend the next twenty on technique.

<<ch13>> ended on a bottleneck that doesn't show up under realistic traffic: a parser that goes quadratic on nesting depth, a validator that goes exponential on a crafted string. We found it by _scaling one adversarial magnitude_ and fitting the response-time curve. This chapter takes that idea and points it at the whole system. Not just "is there a super-linear code path" but "when someone leans on this thing on purpose, does it fall over cleanly or catastrophically, and do the controls we bought to stop them actually work under the load they're supposed to survive?" You are going to generate attacks. You are going to do it to your own systems, on purpose, to find the failure before an adversary does.

[WARNING]
====
Everything in this chapter is dangerous pointed the wrong way. The payloads are designed to exhaust the target; the traffic shapes are designed to trip its defenses. Aimed at a system you don't own or aren't explicitly authorized to test, they are indistinguishable from a real attack — legally and technically. Read the "Rules of engagement" section at the end _first_ if you are the kind of person who acts before finishing the page. This is the one part of the book where "I was just testing" is not a defense.
====

=== The overlap, stated honestly

Every test in this book has been an attack you controlled. A stress test pushes past expected load until the system degrades — that's a volumetric DoS with a friendly intent and an abort switch. A spike test slams idle to peak in ten seconds — that's the traffic shape of a flash crowd or a botnet warm-up. The taxonomy in <<ch01>> was, quietly, a catalog of attack patterns you run against yourself.

What changes in security-and-resilience testing is the _input_, not the mechanism. A load test asks "does the system meet its SLO under the traffic we expect?" A resilience test asks "what does the traffic we _don't_ expect look like, and does the system survive it?" The unexpected traffic comes in two flavors: *volumetric* (more requests than the system was sized for — the domain of rate limiters and autoscaling) and *asymmetric* (a small number of cheap-to-send, expensive-to-serve requests — the domain of complexity attacks and amplification). The second flavor is the interesting one, because it defeats every defense sized around request _count_. Ten requests a second sounds like nothing. Ten ReDoS payloads a second is an outage.

=== Complexity attacks: the weapon from Chapter 13

The asymmetric attack you already know how to find is the algorithmic-complexity blowup. <<ch13>> built the whole method: `loadr payload` generates an adversarial input parameterized by a single magnitude, `${payload:<kind>:$ENVVAR}` embeds it in a request body at the size a sweep axis dictates, and `loadr sweep --complexity` fits the exponent `k` in `response-time ≈ size^k` and fails the build (exit 99) when `k` crosses your bound. A fit near `O(n^1.0)` is linear and safe; `O(n^1.99)` is quadratic and a latent outage.

That's the engine. What <<ch13>> didn't do is enumerate the ammunition. `loadr payload --list` prints eighteen kinds across seven categories, and it's worth knowing the catalog by shape, because each one targets a _different_ super-linear code path and each one hides in a different part of your stack.

=== The adversarial-input catalog

Here's the field guide. Each kind takes a magnitude — a depth, a level count, a byte length — that is the dial you scale to find the bend.

*Nesting → super-linear parsers.* The largest category, and the most common real bug. A recursive-descent parser that's O(n) on input length can be O(n²) on nesting _depth_ if it does per-level work that isn't cheap. `nested-json` (`{"a":{"a":…}}`) and `nested-array` (`[[[…]]]`) hit JSON parsers and their stack limits; `nested-xml` and `nested-html` walk tree-building and sanitizers; `nested-parens` targets expression and filter grammars; `nested-graphql` (`{a{a{…}}}`, default depth 2000) probes query-validation and whether you have a depth limiter at all. The one that catches real production systems is `nested-markdown-blockquote` — a single line of `>>>…` markers — which triggered the goldmark-class super-quadratic blowup that <<ch13>> used as its worked example. A parser that walks that nesting quadratically turns a 64k-character line into a ten-second CPU burn.

*Amplification → small in, huge out.* These are decompression bombs by another name: a tiny document that expands to a giant one _inside_ the parser, before any size limit on the _input_ can help you. `billion-laughs` is the classic XML entity-expansion bomb — nine levels of nested entity definitions expand to roughly 10^9 nodes from a document you could fit in a tweet. `yaml-alias-bomb` is the YAML equivalent: anchor-and-alias references that expand 2^levels, so a twelve-level bomb is 4096 copies and a twenty-four-level bomb is sixteen million. The magnitude here is `levels`, and the caps are deliberately low (12 and 24) because the growth is exponential — one increment doubles the damage.

*Volume → allocation and O(n²) stress.* Big-but-flat inputs that stress allocation, GC, and any accidentally-quadratic per-element loop. `json-array` (N integers), `json-object-keys` (N distinct keys), `csv-rows` (N rows), and `long-string` (one JSON string of N bytes — up to 200MB) are the workhorses for "does this endpoint stream, or does it buffer the whole thing into memory and then walk it twice?"

*ReDoS → catastrophic backtracking.* `redos` emits `'aaaa…!'` — the input that drives a `(a+)+$`-style vulnerable regex into exponential backtracking. This is the asymmetric attack in its purest form: 50 kilobytes of the letter `a` against a naive validator can pin a core for minutes. If any part of your stack validates user input with a hand-written regex — email fields, URL parsers, log scrapers — this is the payload that finds out whether that regex is safe.

*Collision → worst-case hashmaps (HashDoS).* `hash-collision` builds a JSON object whose N keys all hash to the same bucket under 31-based string hashing, turning O(1) map inserts into O(n²). Default count is 65536 keys. This is the attack that made the rounds against web frameworks a decade ago — POST a form with thousands of colliding parameter names and the framework's parameter map degrades to a linked-list walk on every insert. Any endpoint that builds a map keyed by attacker-controlled strings is a candidate.

*Unicode and numeric edge cases.* `zalgo` stacks N combining marks on one base character to blow up normalization, width-calculation, and grapheme-segmentation cost. `bignum` emits a bare integer with N digits to probe arbitrary-precision parse cost — a field you thought was an `int64` that turns out to feed a bignum library scales quadratically on digit count.

[NOTE]
====
Every kind has a hard `max` safety cap, and `loadr payload` refuses to generate past it rather than trying to allocate the machine to death. That cap protects _your_ box, the one generating the payload — it says nothing about what the payload does to the target. A `long-string:200000000` is 200MB you asked for on purpose; the cap is there so you don't type an extra zero and OOM your load generator before the request leaves the building.
====

=== Generating them safely

Two modes, matching two jobs. To prove a path is _reachable_ — a smoke test, a single shot — pipe raw bytes at it. `loadr payload` writes to stdout:

[source,console]
----
# One nested-JSON bomb straight at a parse endpoint. Proves reachability.
$ loadr payload nested-json:20000 | curl -X POST http://localhost:3001/api/parse \
    -H 'Content-Type: application/json' --data-binary @-

# Write a YAML alias bomb to a file to inspect it before firing.
$ loadr payload yaml-alias-bomb:12 -o bomb.yaml
----

To prove it's a _complexity_ problem — and to gate against it forever — embed it in a plan with `${payload:<kind>:$ENVVAR}`, where the magnitude comes from the swept axis, and let the sweep fit the curve. This is `examples/49-payload-complexity.yaml` verbatim: one VU, four iterations, the depth read at request time from `LOADR_SWEEP_DEPTH`.

[source,yaml]
----
scenarios:
  render:
    executor: per-vu-iterations
    vus: 1
    iterations: 4
    flow:
      - request:
          name: render
          method: POST
          url: /api/v1/markup
          headers: { Content-Type: application/json }
          # depth comes from the swept axis; body is generated at request time,
          # never materialised into VU state — a gigabyte payload costs nothing to hold.
          body: '{"Text": "${payload:nested-markdown-blockquote:$LOADR_SWEEP_DEPTH}", "Mode": "markdown"}'
          checks: [ { type: status, equals: 200 } ]
----

[source,console]
----
$ loadr sweep examples/49-payload-complexity.yaml \
    --var depth=4000,8000,16000,32000,64000 \
    --complexity depth --max-exponent 1.2
----

Against a renderer that walks the blockquote nesting quadratically, every 2× in
depth roughly 4×s the p95, and the fit is damning:

[source,console]
----
→ sweeping 5 combination(s) of depth
→ [1/5] depth=4000
  ✓ exit 0 — loadr-sweep/sweep-depth-4000.json
→ [2/5] depth=8000
  ✓ exit 0 — loadr-sweep/sweep-depth-8000.json
...
→ [5/5] depth=64000
  ✓ exit 0 — loadr-sweep/sweep-depth-64000.json

  combo        p50      p95       p99       error rate  rps
  -----------  -------  --------  --------  ----------  -------
  depth=4000   41.20ms  52.90ms   61.00ms   0.00%       18.9/s
  depth=8000   150.4ms  178.2ms   190.1ms   0.00%       5.4/s
  depth=16000  590.7ms  651.0ms   690.4ms   0.00%       1.5/s

----

Read the exit line, not the table. Every combo `✓ exit 0`; every error rate
`0.00%`. Nothing _failed_ — the endpoint just got exponentially slower, which is
precisely the DoS a count-based defense never sees coming. The gate fits a
log-log least-squares line through the five p95 points, lands at `O(n^1.99)` —
quadratic, past the `1.2` bound — and `loadr sweep` **exits 99**, blocking the
merge. A healthy, linear-scaling renderer fits near `O(n^1.03)`, prints
`≈ linear`, and passes. Swap the payload kind and the same gate guards a
different path: a `billion-laughs` body against an XML endpoint fits flat
(`≈ linear`) only if entity expansion is disabled — if it isn't, the exponent
explodes and the exit-99 catches it before production does.

The magic is that the body is generated per request and never held in VU memory, so scaling the magnitude doesn't scale your generator's footprint — a decompression bomb costs the _target_ everything and costs _you_ nothing to send. That asymmetry is the whole point of the attack, and the tooling puts it on your side of the table for once.

The generic recipe is tool-agnostic, and you should hold it separately from any tool: run the same input at several sizes, plot response time against size on log-log axes, read the slope. Slope near 1, linear, safe. Slope near 2, quadratic, a latent DoS. You can do it by hand with a k6 scenario per size feeding a body of the right shape and a spreadsheet fitting the line; loadr just generates the nasty bodies for you and fits the slope so the gate is one command. The reasoning survives the change of tool — only the ergonomics differ.

[TIP]
====
Point the pattern at whichever path you can't afford to have go super-linear: a `redos` body to guard a validator, a `hash-collision` body to guard a parameter map, a `nested-graphql` body to prove your depth-limiter fires, a `billion-laughs` body to prove your XML parser has entity expansion disabled. And because a complexity probe is one VU walking five magnitudes — no sustained load — it belongs on _every PR_, not the overnight sweep. That's the same "move the discovery left" argument from <<ch13>>, and it's the cheapest security test you'll ever write.
====

=== Resource exhaustion: attacking the plumbing, not the code

Complexity attacks exhaust CPU inside a single request. The other family exhausts a _finite pool_ the system depends on, and here the weapon is often the request that _never finishes_ rather than the one that does expensive work.

*Connection and file-descriptor exhaustion.* Every open connection is a file descriptor, and file descriptors are capped (`ulimit -n`, defaults that are often absurdly low). Open more concurrent connections than the target can hold and new ones get `ECONNREFUSED` — a full outage for everyone, caused by connection _count_, not bandwidth. You test this with concurrency, not throughput: an open-model arrival rate with a high `max_vus` and a slow endpoint, watching where connection-establishment latency detaches from request-processing latency (the network signature from <<ch13>>).

*Slowloris — the slow-read/slow-write attack.* The elegant one. Instead of many fast requests, open many connections and send each one a _trickle_ — a header byte every few seconds, a request body dribbled out over minutes. Each connection holds a worker thread or a slot in the accept queue hostage while doing almost nothing. A few hundred slow connections can exhaust a thread-per-connection server's entire pool while its CPU sits at two percent. This is why "requests per second" is the wrong unit for the threat: slowloris is an attack measured in _held connections_, and a rate limiter counting requests waves it straight through.

*Thread-pool and worker starvation.* The software version of FD exhaustion. Every request that blocks — on a slow downstream, a lock, a stalled read — holds a worker. Enough concurrent slow requests and the pool is fully checked out; the N+1st request _queues_, and now fast requests are slow because they're waiting behind slow ones for a worker. You saw the mechanism in <<ch13>> as pool exhaustion; as an attack, you _induce_ it by pointing concurrency at your slowest endpoint and watching a _fast_ endpoint's latency climb in sympathy. If it does, the two share a pool and one can starve the other.

*Memory amplification.* Any request that makes the server allocate more than the request itself costs is a lever: the amplification payloads above, an endpoint that loads an entire result set into memory to paginate it, an upload that's buffered whole. Scale the input and watch RSS; if memory grows super-linearly with input size, you've found an OOM-kill waiting for the right afternoon.

The `faults:` block (`examples/48-chaos.yaml`, and <<ch13>>'s neighbor on chaos) is the cheap way to rehearse the _downstream_ side of this: inject latency jitter and drops into the load loadr generates, and watch whether your own retries and timeouts turn a slow dependency into a thread-pool fire.

[source,yaml]
----
faults:
  latency: { jitter: 200ms, distribution: gaussian }
  drop_rate: 0.02
  drop_mode: before_send

thresholds:
  http_req_duration: [ "slo(95%) < 800ms" ]   # SLO holds despite the jitter
  checks:            [ "rate>0.90" ]           # degrade gracefully, don't collapse
  faults_injected:   [ "count>0" ]             # fail if chaos silently no-oped
----

The `faults_injected` counter earns its place: a chaos run that _passes_ because no chaos was actually injected is the worst kind of green. Threshold on it so a silent no-op fails the run.

=== Validating the defenses under load

Finding the vulnerability is half the job. The other half is proving the _defense_ works — and defenses are exactly the components that behave differently under load than in the unit test. A rate limiter that returns a clean `429` for one client at a desk can melt down when ten thousand clients hit it at once, because the limiter itself is now the hot path.

The test flips the meaning of your gates, exactly as a chaos run does. Under normal load you assert "nothing goes wrong." Against a defense, you assert "the wrong thing gets rejected _correctly_, and the right thing still gets through." That's a two-population check, and loadr's tag selectors are how you judge each population separately — the same `{scenario:...}`-scoped thresholds `examples/48-chaos.yaml` uses to grade the steady traffic while the nemesis is _supposed_ to fail.

*Rate limiters.* Drive an open-model arrival rate _past_ the configured limit and assert three things: legitimate traffic under the limit stays fast, over-limit traffic gets a clean `429` (not a timeout, not a 500), and the limiter's own latency doesn't blow up. A `429` is a _success_ here — the defense working — so you check for it, not against it:

[source,yaml]
----
scenarios:
  flood:
    executor: constant-arrival-rate
    rate: 500              # well past the 100 rps limit
    time_unit: 1s
    duration: 2m
    pre_allocated_vus: 100
    max_vus: 400
    flow:
      - request:
          name: limited
          url: /api/search
          # the limiter is working if over-limit requests get a clean 429,
          # not a 500 and not a hang.
          checks:
            - { type: status, one_of: [200, 429] }
            - { type: duration, name: limiter stays fast, max: 200ms }

thresholds:
  # No 5xx and no timeouts — rejection must be cheap.
  "http_req_failed{status:5xx}": [ "rate<0.001" ]
  http_req_duration:             [ "p(99)<200" ]
----

Drive that at a limiter configured for 100 rps and read the summary. The two
populations show up in one run — the ~100 rps that get through as `200`, and the
~400 rps the limiter sheds as `429` — and every gate is green:

[source,console]
----
$ loadr run flood.yaml

  flood — 1 scenario(s), 2m0s

  checks.....................: 100.00% — ✓ 118402 ✗ 0
    ✓ status is 200 or 429 (59201 / 59201)
    ✓ limiter stays fast (59201 / 59201)
  http_req_duration..........: avg=8.10ms min=1.90ms med=6.40ms
                               max=141.20ms p(90)=14ms p(95)=19ms p(99)=44ms
  http_req_failed............: 0.00% — ✓ 0 ✗ 59201
  http_reqs..................: 59201 (493.3/s)
    ↳ 200: 12043 (100.4/s)   429: 47158 (392.9/s)
  vus........................: value=100 min=100 max=214

  thresholds:
    ✓ http_req_failed{status:5xx}: rate<0.001 (observed: 0.00)
    ✓ http_req_duration: p(99)<200 (observed: 44.10)
----

Two things make this a _pass_, and both are deliberate. The `429`s do not count
as `http_req_failed` — a rejection the plan explicitly accepts (`one_of [200,
429]`) is a check success, not a transport failure, so the error rate stays
`0.00%` even though four out of five requests were turned away. And the
`{status:5xx}` selector isolates the population that _would_ be a failure:
`rate<0.001` fires only if the limiter starts returning 500s instead of shedding
cleanly. The p99 of `44ms` is the third assertion earning its keep — the limiter
is rejecting in tens of milliseconds, not holding connections.

Now break the limiter — swap it for one that opens a database transaction on
every request before deciding to reject — and the same plan turns red:

[source,console]
----
  http_reqs..................: 21847 (182.1/s)
    ↳ 200: 9204 (76.7/s)   429: 8971 (74.8/s)   503: 3672 (30.6/s)
  http_req_duration..........: avg=1.42s ... p(99)=8.90s

  thresholds:
    ✗ http_req_failed{status:5xx}: rate<0.001 (observed: 0.168)
    ✗ http_req_duration: p(99)<200 (observed: 8900.00)
----

Throughput collapsed from 493 rps to 182, the p99 ran to nine seconds, and 16.8%
of requests are `503`s — the limiter became the bottleneck it was bought to
prevent. Both gates fail, and the run exits non-zero. That is the whole test:
the defense must reject the wrong traffic _cheaply_, and the two tag-scoped
thresholds are what tell a healthy shield from one you can DoS through.

If `429`s arrive in single-digit milliseconds while 5xx stays at zero, the limiter is shedding load correctly. If you see 500s or the p99 climbing into seconds, the limiter is _adding_ latency under attack — it's become a bottleneck instead of a shield, and you've found a way to DoS the thing that was supposed to prevent DoS.

*Circuit breakers and load shedding.* A circuit breaker should trip when a downstream fails and fail _fast_ — return an error in a millisecond rather than holding a thread for a 30-second timeout. Point the `faults:` block or a fault-injecting scenario at the dependency and assert that when it goes bad, the caller's latency stays _low_ (fast rejection) rather than climbing to the timeout. loadr's own abort threshold — `abort_on_fail: true` with `delay_abort_eval` — is a circuit breaker for the _test_: if error rate explodes, the run stops instead of burning the target. That same `examples/48-chaos.yaml` pattern is the model for what you're asserting about the _system's_ breaker:

[source,yaml]
----
thresholds:
  "http_req_failed{scenario:steady_traffic}":
    - { threshold: "rate<0.05", abort_on_fail: true, delay_abort_eval: 20s }
  "http_req_duration{scenario:steady_traffic}": [ "p(95)<800" ]
----

*Autoscaling as a DoS absorber.* Autoscaling is a defense with a _time constant_: it works if it can add capacity faster than the attack adds load. A spike test (`examples/04-spike-test.yaml`) is the exact tool — idle baseline, sudden 10× jump, sustained spike, recovery — and the question is whether the scaler catches up before the SLO breaks and the queue collapses. Watch the gap between the spike and the recovery: if latency stays broken for the two minutes it takes new instances to come up, an attacker who spikes for ninety seconds and stops has won without you ever adding useful capacity — and left you paying for instances that booted just in time to be idle.

*WAFs.* A web application firewall is a per-request cost on the hot path and a component that can itself be exhausted. Test it two ways: send the adversarial payloads and confirm it _blocks_ them (a fast `403`), and send high-volume _legitimate_ traffic and confirm its inspection cost doesn't dominate your latency budget. A WAF that adds 40ms of inspection to a 60ms request has eaten your latency SLO to buy security — a real trade, but one you should make with the number in front of you.

=== Auth under attack

Authentication is where asymmetry lives in plain sight. Verifying a token, checking a password hash, minting a session — these are deliberately _expensive_ (a bcrypt verify is designed to cost milliseconds), which makes the auth path a natural amplification target. An unauthenticated attacker can force expensive work with nothing but a stream of bad credentials.

*Credential-stuffing-shaped load.* A flood of login attempts with plausible-but-wrong credentials, each forcing a full password-hash comparison. `examples/05-data-driven.yaml`'s feeder pattern supplies the username/password pairs; the shape is a high arrival rate at `/login` with unique bodies. Assert that legitimate logins interleaved with the flood still succeed within SLO, and that the hash-verify cost doesn't starve the rest of the service. If your login endpoint shares a thread pool with everything else, credential stuffing is a whole-service DoS.

*Token-minting storms.* The auth-token pattern from `examples/36-auth-tokens.yaml` mints a token per VU and refreshes before expiry, tracking a `token_refreshes` counter. Turn the dial up: many VUs all minting simultaneously is a thundering herd at the token endpoint, and the signing cost (especially asymmetric RS256 signing) can dominate. And the _verify_ side matters as much — if every downstream request re-verifies a JWT signature, you've put a signature check on the hot path. Scale concurrent authed traffic and watch whether verification cost tracks request rate; if it does, a valid token used aggressively is its own amplification vector.

=== Amplification and fan-out

The general shape behind half this chapter: one cheap request that triggers expensive downstream work. A search query that fans out to twenty backend services. An API call that triggers a webhook to a third party. A cache-miss that stampedes a cold database. The attacker sends one small request; your system does a hundred small expensive things. The tail-latency arithmetic from <<ch01>> is the same math read as a weapon — if one request fans out to 100 downstream calls, the attacker doesn't need volume, they need to reliably hit the fan-out.

Test it by measuring the _ratio_: instrument downstream call count per inbound request (the observability wiring from <<ch11>>), then find the input that maximizes it. An endpoint where one request can cause unbounded downstream work — a wildcard search, an unpaginated list, a recursive expand — is an amplification vulnerability whether or not anyone's malicious, because your _own_ retry storm (the congestive collapse from <<ch01>>) will find it eventually.

=== Measuring resilience, not just failure

The point of all this is not to prove the system breaks. Given enough pressure, everything breaks; that's not a finding, it's physics. The finding is _how_ it breaks. There are two ways, and the difference is the entire game.

*Graceful degradation* means that as load climbs past capacity, the system sheds the excess cleanly: over-limit requests get a fast `429`, non-essential features turn off, the core path stays within SLO for the traffic it does accept. Throughput plateaus. Errors are bounded and _cheap_. The `checks: [ "rate>0.90" ]` threshold under chaos is the assertion — "most requests still succeed" — and it's the shape of a system that survives its worst day.

*Congestive collapse* means throughput goes _down_ as load goes up — the death spiral from <<ch01>>. Requests pile up, each holding resources longer, timeouts fire, retries pile on, and the system does less useful work under more load until it does none. The signature is throughput that peaks and then falls while latency runs away to the timeout. If your stress test shows that curve, you don't have a capacity problem you can autoscale out of; you have a stability problem, and adding capacity just moves the cliff.

And then _recovery_. Take the load away and start the clock. A resilient system drains its queues and returns to baseline in seconds. A fragile one stays wedged — queues that never drain, connections stuck in `TIME_WAIT`, a cache that stampedes on the way back up. The recovery tail of a spike test is where you learn which one you have, and it's the number most teams forget to look at because the run "passed" the moment traffic stopped. It didn't. The test isn't over until the system is back.

=== Rules of engagement

This is the part you read first. The techniques in this chapter are indistinguishable from an attack, so the discipline that separates a security test from a crime is entirely _procedural_. There is no clever flag that makes it safe; there is only doing it right.

*Authorization, in writing, before anything.* You test systems you own or have explicit, documented authorization to test. Not "my manager said it's fine" — a written scope: which systems, which windows, which techniques, who to call when it goes wrong. If a third party runs any part of the stack (a cloud provider, a payment processor, a CDN), their acceptable-use policy governs load testing against it, and violating it can get your whole account terminated. Check before you fire.

*Blast radius, contained.* A disposable, isolated environment — never shared staging that other teams depend on, and production only under a formal, coordinated program with the people who carry the pager in the room. Start every magnitude _small_ and ramp: the first ReDoS payload is a hundred bytes, not a hundred kilobytes, and you scale up only once you've seen the target handle the small one cleanly. Keep the abort threshold armed (`abort_on_fail`) so a run that starts an outage _stops itself_.

*Coordinate.* Tell the security team before you generate attack traffic, or your test becomes their 3am incident and you become the reason they stopped trusting the on-call channel. Tell whoever watches the dashboards. A resilience test that triggers a real incident response is a failure of coordination, not a success of testing — though if your detection _didn't_ fire, that's a finding too, and one worth writing down.

The line is intent plus authorization plus containment. Miss any of the three and you are not testing; you are attacking, and the tooling — which, remember, cannot tell the difference — will help you do it just as effectively either way. The whole of this chapter, and the next one (<<ch15>>) where these probes go into CI to run unattended, rests on getting those three right every single time.

.Key takeaways
****
* A load test and a DoS are the *same mechanism, different intent* — the tool can't tell them apart, so the discipline is procedural: authorization, blast radius, coordination, every time.
* The dangerous attacks are *asymmetric*: cheap to send, expensive to serve. They defeat every defense sized around request _count_. Complexity blowups, amplification bombs, and slowloris all trade a trickle of input for an outage.
* Know the *catalog by shape*: nesting for parsers, amplification for expansion bombs, `redos` for validators, `hash-collision` for parameter maps, volume for allocators. Each targets a different super-linear path.
* *Generate them safely*: bodies stream at request time and never bloat your generator, so the asymmetry works for you; every kind has a safety cap that protects the _generator_, not the target.
* *Validate defenses under load, not at a desk.* A rate limiter must reject with a fast `429` and no 5xx; a circuit breaker must fail _fast_, not hold a thread to the timeout; autoscaling must add capacity faster than the attack adds load.
* Measure *how it breaks, not that it breaks*: graceful degradation (throughput plateaus, errors bounded and cheap) versus congestive collapse (throughput falls as load rises). And time the *recovery* — the test isn't over until the system is back.
****

[.fieldcard]
.Field Card — Attack & Resilience Testing
****
Reach for these when you're stress-testing a system on purpose — and only one you're authorized to break.

Prove a path is reachable:: `loadr payload <kind>:<n>` piped at the endpoint — a single shot.
Prove it's a complexity bug:: embed `${payload:<kind>:$LOADR_SWEEP_<AXIS>}`, then `sweep --complexity` and gate the exponent.
Test a rate limiter:: open-model `constant-arrival-rate` past the limit; assert `status one_of [200,429]` and `{status:5xx} rate<0.001`.
Test a breaker / degradation:: fault-inject, then `abort_on_fail` + scenario-scoped `checks rate>0.90`.

[source,console]
----
loadr payload --list                       # 18 kinds, 7 categories, each with a cap
loadr payload nested-json:20000 | curl -X POST … --data-binary @-
loadr sweep plan.yaml --var depth=4000,8000,16000,32000,64000 \
      --complexity depth --max-exponent 1.2   # exit 99 = super-linear, merge blocked
----

*Remember:* asymmetric beats volumetric — 10 ReDoS/s is an outage; rate limiters count requests and wave slowloris through. Start every magnitude small. A `429` is a pass. Amplification caps protect *your* box, not the target. Time the recovery.
